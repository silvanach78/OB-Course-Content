Organizational Culture and Change

Introduction

Organizational culture is a system of shared assumptions, values, and beliefs that helps employees understand what behavior is appropriate or inappropriate. Culture shapes how people interact, how decisions are made, how employees respond to customers, and how organizations adapt to change. Culture is powerful because it often operates below conscious awareness. Employees may not notice their own organization’s culture until they compare it with another workplace that has different assumptions about communication, hierarchy, work-life boundaries, risk, or performance expectations.1Chatman, J. A., & Cha, S. E. (2003). Leading by leveraging culture. California Management Review, 45, 19–34; Kerr, J., & Slocum, J. W., Jr. (2005). Managing corporate culture through reward systems. Academy of Management Executive, 19, 130–138.

Culture and change belong together. Culture helps explain “how things are done around here,” while organizational change explains what happens when those ways of working must shift. Organizations change because of technology, globalization, workforce demographics, market conditions, growth, poor performance, mergers, or leadership transitions. However, any change effort must confront the existing culture, because people do not simply adopt new behaviors because leaders announce them. They must let go of old routines, learn new expectations, and believe the change is necessary and workable.

The framework below shows why organizational culture and change must be diagnosed at multiple levels. Individual characteristics such as political skill and social capital affect how employees navigate culture, build relationships, and respond to change. Situational factors such as organizational culture, leader’s values and behaviors, HR practices and organizational policies, rewards, technology, market conditions, growth, and poor performance shape the processes through which culture is maintained or changed. These processes affect individual, group, and organizational outcomes.

Instructor's Note. Be able to explain how culture shapes behavior, how culture is maintained, why culture can become a barrier to change, and how managers can use change models to increase commitment rather than resistance.

Why Organizational Culture Matters

Culture can be one of an organization’s strongest assets, but it can also become one of its largest liabilities. When culture is rare, difficult to imitate, and aligned with the organization’s environment, it can become a source of sustained competitive advantage. A strong service culture, for example, can guide employees to solve customer problems even when no rule tells them exactly what to do. A strong safety culture can encourage employees to identify hazards and protect coworkers before accidents occur.2Barney, J. B. (1986). Organizational culture: Can it be a source of sustained competitive advantage? Academy of Management Review, 11, 656–665; Kotter, J. P., & Heskett, J. L. (1992). Corporate culture and performance. Free Press.

Culture matters because it operates as a control mechanism. Formal policies cannot anticipate every situation employees face. Culture fills that gap by signaling priorities. If a company’s culture emphasizes customer service, employees may issue a refund, escalate a concern, or solve a customer problem without waiting for permission. If the culture emphasizes cost control, employees may prioritize efficiency and standardization. Neither value is automatically better; the effectiveness of culture depends on whether the values fit the strategy and environment.

Culture can also become dangerous when it reinforces the wrong behaviors. A highly competitive, results-only culture may encourage employees to hit targets at any cost. A hierarchy culture may support reliability in a regulated environment but slow adaptation in a fast-changing market. The managerial question is therefore not whether a culture is “strong,” but whether it is aligned, ethical, and adaptable.

Levels and Visible Elements of Culture

Schein’s model describes culture at three levels: artifacts, values, and basic assumptions. Artifacts are visible aspects of culture, such as office layout, dress code, rituals, stories, policies, language, and symbols. Values are shared principles and goals that employees say matter, such as innovation, customer service, teamwork, safety, or efficiency. Basic assumptions are deeper, taken-for-granted beliefs about reality and human behavior, such as whether employees can be trusted, whether mistakes should be punished, or whether disagreement is respectful or disloyal.3Schein, E. H. (1992). Organizational culture and leadership. Jossey-Bass.

Artifacts are the easiest to observe but the hardest to interpret accurately. A casual dress code may indicate creativity, cost control, informality, or simply industry norms. To understand culture, managers must connect artifacts to values and assumptions. For example, Mayo Clinic’s mission that “the needs of the patient come first” is meaningful because its compensation and referral practices support patient-centered care. In contrast, Enron’s values statements about respect and integrity became meaningless because they did not match actual behavior and incentives.

Culture is also communicated through rituals, policies, physical layout, and stories. Mary Kay award ceremonies reinforce achievement and enthusiasm. Walmart’s Saturday morning meetings reinforced accountability, performance orientation, and quick action. Nike’s campus communicates energy, athletics, and competitiveness, while Walmart’s modest corporate offices historically communicated thrift. Stories, such as 3M’s Post-it note story, transmit values like experimentation, persistence, and proactive problem solving.

Culture Dimensions and Culture Types

Because organizational culture is complex, managers need frameworks that help diagnose cultural patterns. Two useful frameworks are the Organizational Culture Profile (OCP) and the Competing Values Framework (CVF). The OCP describes specific cultural dimensions, while the CVF organizes broader cultural types around competing priorities.4,5O’Reilly, C. A., Chatman, J., & Caldwell, D. F. (1991). People and organizational culture: A profile comparison approach to assessing person–organization fit. Academy of Management Journal, 34(3), 487–516.; Cameron, K. S., & Quinn, R. E. (2011). Diagnosing and changing organizational culture: Based on the competing values framework. Jossey-Bass.

Organizational Culture Profile

The OCP identifies cultural dimensions such as innovation, aggressiveness, outcome orientation, stability, people orientation, team orientation, and detail orientation. These dimensions are useful because they move culture from a vague label to observable priorities. For example, an outcome-oriented culture may focus heavily on performance metrics and measurable results, while a people-oriented culture may emphasize fairness, support, and employee well-being.4O’Reilly, C. A., Chatman, J., & Caldwell, D. F. (1991). People and organizational culture: A profile comparison approach to assessing person–organization fit. Academy of Management Journal, 34(3), 487–516.

Competing Values Framework

The Competing Values Framework (CVF), The CVF organizes culture along two key dimensions. The first dimension ranges from flexibility and adaptability to stability and control, capturing whether an organization emphasizes change or consistency. The second dimension contrasts an internal focus (integration, unity, and cohesion) with an external focus (competition, differentiation, and positioning). Together, these dimensions form four quadrants, each representing a distinct definition of organizational effectiveness.5Cameron, K. S., & Quinn, R. E. (2011). Diagnosing and changing organizational culture: Based on the competing values framework. Jossey-Bass.

A key insight of this framework is that these values are inherently competing. Flexibility conflicts with stability, and internal focus conflicts with external competitiveness. As a result, cultures positioned diagonally from each other reflect opposing assumptions. For example, a Collaborate (Clan) culture emphasizes relationships and cohesion, while a Compete (Market) culture emphasizes performance and results. Similarly, a Create (Adhocracy) culture, focused on innovation, contrasts with a Control (Hierarchy) culture, focused on consistency and efficiency. This means that strengthening one cultural dimension often requires trade-offs with others.5Cameron, K. S., & Quinn, R. E. (2011). Diagnosing and changing organizational culture: Based on the competing values framework. Jossey-Bass.

The four core culture types are described below. The comparison table (Table 6.3) that follows summarizes key differences across dimensions such as leadership, values, and success criteria, allowing for direct comparison.

Clan Culture (Collaborate)

Clan cultures emphasize collaboration, cohesion, and a strong sense of community. Organizations with this culture focus on involvement, participation, and long-term commitment, often seeking to be the employer of choice. Employees tend to share values, communicate openly, and identify strongly with the organization.

From a management perspective, clan cultures create a friendly and supportive work environment where teamwork and consensus are emphasized. Leaders act as mentors or facilitators, focusing on building trust and strengthening relationships. This approach fosters loyalty and long-term employee development, which can become a competitive advantage through a strong organizational identity.

Success in clan cultures is defined not only by performance but also by meeting stakeholder needs and caring for people, including employees and customers, who may be viewed as part of an extended community. Organizations such as Chick-fil-A, as well as many nonprofits and educational institutions, exemplify this culture.

However, if taken too far, clan cultures can become overly permissive, where harmony is prioritized at the expense of accountability. When this occurs, performance standards may weaken, and the organization may struggle to achieve measurable results.

Adhocracy Culture (Create)

Adhocracy cultures emphasize innovation, creativity, and adaptability. These organizations are future-oriented and thrive in dynamic, uncertain environments. Employees are encouraged to experiment, take risks, and generate new ideas, making this culture common in start-ups and technology-driven industries such as Apple, Dyson, and Samsung.

Managers in adhocracy cultures create a dynamic and entrepreneurial environment that values initiative and autonomy. Leadership is characterized by vision, risk-taking, and innovation, with leaders and employees sharing a commitment to experimentation and growth. The organization’s competitive advantage comes from its ability to develop new products, services, and capabilities.

Success is defined by innovation outcomes, such as introducing new products or entering new markets. This culture is particularly effective in industries where adaptability and creativity are critical to survival.

However, the same flexibility that drives innovation can create problems if not balanced. When taken to an extreme, adhocracy cultures can become chaotic and unfocused, with too many ideas and insufficient structure. This can lead to inefficiency, lack of coordination, and difficulty achieving consistent results.

Market Culture (Compete)

Market cultures emphasize competition, performance, and goal achievement. These organizations focus on external positioning, results, and winning in the marketplace. Employees are expected to be driven, productive, and focused on achieving targets, making this culture common in industries such as consulting, sales, marketing, and manufacturing. Amazon is often cited as an example.

Managers in market cultures create a high-performance environment with clear expectations and strong accountability. Leadership is characterized by competitiveness, productivity, and a results-oriented mindset, with leaders acting as drivers who push for performance improvements.

The organization’s values center on profitability, market share, speed, and measurable outcomes. Success is defined in terms of achieving goals, outperforming competitors, and delivering strong performance metrics. The competitive advantage lies in the ability to respond quickly to market demands and deliver results efficiently.

However, when market cultures become too dominant, they can create excessive pressure, internal conflict, and self-interest. A narrow focus on results may lead to neglect of employee well-being, collaboration, or ethical considerations, increasing the risk of burnout and dysfunctional behavior.

Hierarchy Culture (Control)

Hierarchy cultures emphasize stability, efficiency, and structured processes. These organizations rely on formal rules, procedures, and clear roles to ensure predictable and reliable performance. This culture is common in industries where consistency, safety, and compliance are critical, such as government, banking, pharmaceuticals, and transportation. Examples include McDonald’s, Walmart, and large government institutions.

Managers in hierarchy cultures create a formalized and structured environment where procedures guide behavior and reduce uncertainty. Leadership focuses on coordination, organization, and process optimization, ensuring that operations run smoothly and efficiently.

The organization values consistency, quality, punctuality, and cost control, with success defined by reliable performance, efficiency, and low variability. This culture supports long-term stability and is particularly effective in environments where errors must be minimized.

However, when taken to an extreme, hierarchy cultures can become overly bureaucratic, leading to excessive rules, slow decision-making, and resistance to change. This can result in stagnation and reduced innovation, especially in rapidly changing environments.

Two additional culture types are especially important in applied settings. A service culture emphasizes proactive problem solving for customers. In service cultures, employees are trained and empowered to respond to customer needs, as seen in examples such as Ritz-Carlton, Southwest Airlines, and Umpqua Bank. A safety culture emphasizes safe behaviors, hazard identification, and prevention. Safety culture is critical in industries where mistakes can cause serious harm, such as energy, aviation, construction, health care, and manufacturing.

Culture Strength, Subcultures, and Countercultures

A strong culture is one in which members widely share and understand the organization’s values. Strong cultures create consistency, identity, and behavioral guidance. In stable environments, strong cultures can produce reliable performance because employees know what is expected. However, strong cultures are not always beneficial. If the shared values are unethical, misaligned with strategy, or poorly suited to a changing environment, a strong culture can become a liability.6Sorensen, J. B. (2002). The strength of corporate culture and the reliability of firm performance. Administrative Science Quarterly, 47, 70–91.

Strong cultures can also resist change. Home Depot’s culture, for example, was historically decentralized and allowed store-level autonomy. When Robert Nardelli attempted to centralize decision-making and use more data-driven systems, he achieved some financial results but also faced resistance and criticism because the changes challenged deeply held cultural assumptions.11Charan, R. (2006, April). Home Depot’s blueprint for culture change. Harvard Business Review, 84(4), 60–70.

Organizations also rarely have only one culture. Subcultures may develop across departments, locations, occupations, or levels. Marketing may value creativity and external responsiveness, while manufacturing may value precision, consistency, and efficiency. Subcultures are not necessarily harmful; they may help different parts of the organization respond to different demands. Problems arise when subcultures become misaligned or undermine coordination.

A counterculture is a subculture whose values oppose the dominant culture. A counterculture can be useful if it protects innovation, ethical dissent, or new ways of thinking. It can also be threatening if it undermines strategic alignment or creates conflict. Managers need to diagnose whether a counterculture is a source of renewal or a sign of destructive fragmentation.

Subcultures influence group/team dynamics because employees in different departments or occupations may develop different assumptions about what matters most. These differences can improve problem solving when groups coordinate well, but they can also create group cohesion/conflict problems when subcultures compete or distrust one another.

How Culture Is Created and Maintained

Culture is created as organizations solve external and internal problems. If a way of working helps the organization survive or succeed, it becomes repeated, taught, and eventually taken for granted. Founders often play a central role because their values shape early hiring, structures, routines, and stories. Ben & Jerry’s reflects the social values of its founders, while Microsoft’s early competitiveness is often connected to Bill Gates’s own competitive orientation. Industry demands also shape culture. Banking, health care, and nuclear power often require more rules and stability, while high-tech industries often require speed, experimentation, and flexibility.3Schein, E. H. (1992). Organizational culture and leadership. Jossey-Bass.

Culture maintenance also depends on employees’ political skill and social capital. Employees with strong networks often learn informal expectations faster, gain access to mentors, and understand how influence works inside the organization. The fit between employees and the organization is especially important. Person–organization fit, or P–O fit, occurs when an employee’s values and preferences align with the organization’s culture. Strong fit can improve workplace attitudes, commitment, and adjustment, while poor fit can increase dissatisfaction and turnover.4,9O’Reilly, C. A., Chatman, J., & Caldwell, D. F. (1991). Changing Organizational Culture

Culture change is difficult because culture is part of an organization’s history and identity. Employees may resist change because old assumptions once helped the organization succeed. Culture change is more likely when the organization faces a crisis, declining performance, new competition, a merger, or a strategic shift that makes the old culture visibly ineffective.

Successful culture change requires more than slogans or updated mission statements. It requires consistent changes in leadership behavior, systems, symbols, rewards, and everyday practices. Research and case studies identify several actions that increase the likelihood of success.10Schein, E. H. (1990). Organizational culture. American Psychologist, 45, 109–119; Cummings, T. G., & Worley, C. G. (2015). Organization development and change (10th ed.). Cengage Learning; Kotter, J. P. (1996). Leading change. Harvard Business School Press.

Culture change is especially difficult during mergers and acquisitions because organizations bring different histories, assumptions, and practices. The Daimler-Chrysler merger illustrates this problem. Daimler had a hierarchical engineering culture with long work hours and elite status assumptions, while Chrysler had a more autonomous sales-oriented culture. Leaders must also watch for inconsistency. If the organization says it values safety but rewards speed over hazard reporting, employees will learn that speed matters more. If it says it values ethics but promotes high performers who mistreat others, employees will learn that ethics are optional. Culture change succeeds only when the new values are embedded in daily decisions, systems, and consequences.

Exam tip. Culture change is systemic. It requires changing leadership behavior, selection, onboarding, rewards, symbols, and stories. A new mission statement alone is not culture change.

Organizational Change and Its Causes

Organizational change is the movement of an organization from one state of affairs to another. Change may involve structure, strategy, policies, procedures, technology, culture, or work processes. It may be planned years in advance or forced by environmental shifts. It may be radical and transform how the organization operates, or incremental and slowly alter how work is done. Regardless of the type, change requires people to let go of old routines and adjust to new expectations, which makes change fundamentally a people-management challenge.17Cummings, T. G., & Worley, C. G. (2015). Organization development and change (10th ed.). Cengage Learning.

TechnologyNew technology can disrupt products, processes, business models, and customer expectations.The music industry struggled when file sharing disrupted CD sales; Apple’s iTunes created a new digital distribution model.
GlobalizationOrganizations must adapt to global competition, outsourcing, global teams, and different institutional environments.Software development and service work moved to countries such as India, requiring firms to manage global workflows and employee stress from job relocation.

Leadership ChangeNew CEOs or top management teams often change strategy, structure, and culture.Long-tenured leaders may continue old formulas for success, while new leaders may be more willing to question them.

Environmental change does not automatically change an organization. Managers must notice the change, interpret it correctly, and act. Successful companies may be especially vulnerable because past success can create overconfidence and inertia. The phrase “nothing fails like success” captures this risk: what made the organization successful in the past may become the reason it fails to adapt.

These pressures for change matter because they affect organizational outcomes. A company that responds effectively to technological, market, or competitive pressure may increase innovation, efficiency, and agility. A company that ignores these pressures may experience declining financial performance, weaker customer satisfaction, and reduced organizational effectiveness.17Cummings, T. G., & Worley, C. G. (2015). Organization development and change (10th ed.). Cengage Learning.

Resistance to Change

Change is often necessary for survival, but employees do not always welcome it. Reactions to change may range from active resistance to commitment. Active resistance includes open opposition or sabotage. Passive resistance includes quiet dislike, stress, withdrawal, or searching for another job without openly objecting. Compliance means going along with the change with little enthusiasm. Commitment means actively supporting the change and encouraging others to support it.

Resistance can create serious organizational and personal consequences. Organizations may lose time, energy, and adaptability. Employees who react negatively to change may experience stress, negative emotions, more sick time, and higher turnover intentions. However, resistance is not always bad. It may provide useful feedback. Some of the most committed employees may resist because they believe the change will harm the organization. Managers should therefore diagnose resistance rather than simply blame it on disloyalty.19Oreg, S., Vakola, M., & Armenakis, A. (2011). Change recipients’ reactions to organizational change: A 60-year review of quantitative studies. The Journal of Applied Behavioral Science, 47(4), 461–524.

The QWERTY keyboard provides a useful example of resistance to change. The Dvorak keyboard is more efficient, but QWERTY remained dominant because typists, teachers, manufacturers, and systems had invested in the old design. This shows that the best technical solution does not always win. Change also depends on habits, switching costs, power, training, and stakeholder resistance.

Resistance also affects outcomes at multiple levels. At the individual level, poorly managed change can damage workplace attitudes, increase stress, and increase turnover. At the group level, it can reduce group performance or increase conflict. At the organizational level, resistance can slow implementation, reduce efficiency, and weaken agility.19Oreg, S., Vakola, M., & Armenakis, A. (2011). Change recipients’ reactions to organizational change: A 60-year review of quantitative studies. The Journal of Applied Behavioral Science, 47(4), 461–524.

Planning and Executing Change

This framework shows how Lewin’s model and Kotter’s model complement each other. Lewin’s Three-Step Model provides the broad logic of change: prepare people for change, move to new behaviors, and reinforce those behaviors so they become normal. Kotter’s Eight-Step Model provides more detailed actions managers can use within those stages, including creating a sense of urgency, building a guiding coalition, communicating a vision for change, removing obstacles, creating small wins, consolidating gains, and anchoring change in the culture.18Lewin, K. (1951). Field theory in social science. Harper; Kotter, J. P. (1996). Leading change. Harvard Business School Press.

Two widely used models help managers plan change: Lewin’s three-step model and Kotter’s eight-step model. Lewin provides a broad framework: unfreeze, move, and refreeze. Kotter provides more detailed steps that fit within those stages. Together, the models show that successful change requires preparation, implementation, and long-term reinforcement.18Lewin, K. (1951). Field theory in social science. Harper; Kotter, J. P. (1996). Leading change. Harvard Business School Press.

Unfreezing requires creating readiness. Employees must understand why the current way is no longer adequate. Establishing urgency does not mean creating panic, but it does require explaining the consequences of inaction. A guiding coalition is also important because change rarely succeeds through formal authority alone. Leaders need influential employees and managers who can build support through formal and informal networks.18Lewin, K. (1951). Field theory in social science. Harper; Kotter, J. P. (1996). Leading change. Harvard Business School Press.

Moving requires a clear vision, communication, participation, and obstacle removal. Employees need to know what is changing, why it is changing, what will be expected of them, and how they will be supported. Communication reduces uncertainty, but communication alone is insufficient if systems, structures, or skills prevent the change from working. Small wins are especially useful because they show progress, build confidence, and increase credibility.18Lewin, K. (1951). Field theory in social science. Harper; Kotter, J. P. (1996). Leading change. Harvard Business School Press.

Refreezing means making the change stick. New practices must be reinforced through rewards, routines, policies, leadership behavior, and culture. Organizations often fail by declaring victory too early. Instead, managers should consolidate gains, continue improving, and anchor new behaviors so they become normal. In other words, change becomes successful when it is no longer treated as a special initiative, but as “the way we do things here.”18Lewin, K. (1951). Field theory in social science. Harper; Kotter, J. P. (1996). Leading change. Harvard Business School Press.

Effective change management also supports employee development. During change, employees may need training, coaching, mentoring, and opportunities to practice new behaviors. These supports can improve task performance, strengthen career outcomes, and make the change more sustainable.

Ethics, National Culture, and Change

Ethics is inseparable from culture and change. Ethics training alone is unlikely to work if the culture rewards unethical behavior or if leaders contradict the values they claim to support. Leaders shape ethical culture through role modeling, decisions, rewards, and reactions to misconduct. If leaders create extremely difficult goals and reward only results, employees may learn that unethical behavior is acceptable as long as targets are met. Enron and WorldCom illustrate the risks of cultures where espoused values and actual incentives diverge.20Gebler, D. (2006, May). Creating an ethical culture. Strategic Finance, 87, 28–34; Arogyaswamy, B., & Byles, C. H. (1987). Organizational culture: Internal and external fits. Journal of Management, 13, 647–658.

National culture also influences organizational culture. When founders create organizations, they often transmit values shaped by their broader society. A culture emphasizing competitiveness may produce more market-oriented organizations, while a culture emphasizing harmony may support more team-oriented practices. National culture can also shape architecture, hierarchy, communication patterns, and expectations about authority. This means that creating the “same” culture globally can be difficult because local norms influence how employees interpret values and practices.20Gebler, D. (2006, May). Creating an ethical culture. Strategic Finance, 87, 28–34; Arogyaswamy, B., & Byles, C. H. (1987). Organizational culture: Internal and external fits. Journal of Management, 13, 647–658.

For managers, the global implication is clear: culture change cannot simply be copied and pasted across locations. A practice that signals empowerment in one country may create uncertainty in another. A practice that signals respect for hierarchy in one context may feel restrictive in another. Effective managers connect organizational values to local realities while still preserving core ethical principles.

Ethical culture and global context also affect customer and stakeholder outcomes. When employees see that leaders behave consistently with stated values, the organization is more likely to maintain trust. When local cultural differences are ignored, change initiatives may be interpreted as disrespectful or unrealistic. These interpretations can affect customer satisfaction, employee trust, and the organization’s reputation.20Gebler, D. (2006, May). Creating an ethical culture. Strategic Finance, 87, 28–34; Arogyaswamy, B., & Byles, C. H. (1987). Organizational culture: Internal and external fits. Journal of Management, 13, 647–658.

Key Takeaways

  • Organizational culture is a system of shared assumptions, values, and beliefs that guides behavior.
  • Culture can be a competitive advantage when it is rare, difficult to imitate, ethical, and aligned with the environment.
  • Culture operates at three levels: artifacts, values, and basic assumptions.
  • Artifacts are visible, but assumptions are often the deepest drivers of behavior.
  • The Competing Values Framework shows that culture involves trade-offs between flexibility and stability, and internal and external focus.
  • Strong cultures create consistency, but they can also produce resistance to change and ethical blind spots.
  • Culture is maintained through founder values, industry demands, ASA, onboarding, leadership, and reward systems.
  • Organizational change may be caused by demographics, technology, globalization, market conditions, growth, poor performance, or leadership transitions.
  • Resistance to change can be active, passive, compliance-based, or replaced by true commitment.
  • Resistance may come from habits, uncertainty, fear of failure, change fatigue, or perceived loss of power.
  • Lewin and Kotter both show that successful change requires preparation, implementation, and long-term reinforcement.
  • Ethical and national culture shape both organizational culture and the success of change efforts.

Managerial Insight

Managers shape culture through what they hire for, what they reward, what they tolerate, what they measure, and how they respond to critical events. Culture cannot be changed simply by rewriting values statements. Employees watch what leaders actually do, which behaviors are rewarded, and whether stated values match daily decisions.

Culture change is especially difficult because it asks people to change routines, assumptions, identities, and power relationships. Managers should therefore treat resistance as information, not merely as a problem. Some resistance reveals poor communication, weak training, unrealistic timelines, or valid concerns about whether the change will work.

The most effective managers connect culture and change. They diagnose whether the existing culture supports or blocks strategy, communicate why change is necessary, involve credible allies, reduce uncertainty, create small wins, and reinforce new behaviors through systems and rewards. Ultimately, culture change succeeds when the new behaviors become embedded in the organization’s everyday routines and assumptions.
