First Post

Google operates internationally in many countries, including China. In 2006, Google launched a Chinese-language search engine in an attempt to expand into the Chinese market. However, the company faced major challenges because China’s government requires internet companies to comply with strict censorship laws. According to the Module 1 presentation, Google’s compliance with censorship conflicted with the company’s informal motto, “Don’t be evil,” and the company later stopped censoring search results, which contributed to China restricting Google’s operations in the country.

In a previous organization where I worked, management became concerned about increasing absenteeism and declining customer service scores. Senior leaders initially believed that employees were becoming less committed to the organization and discussed implementing stricter attendance policies. However, after conducting employee surveys and focus groups, they discovered that the real issue was a combination of excessive workload and staffing shortages following several employee departures.

This example illustrates the importance of understanding the relationship between job satisfaction, organizational commitment, and workplace behaviors. According to this week's readings, employees who are dissatisfied with aspects of their jobs are more likely to engage in withdrawal behaviors such as absenteeism and turnover. In this case, management initially focused on the behavior itself rather than examining the underlying causes.

The situation also demonstrates how managers can fall victim to the fundamental attribution error. Leaders assumed employees were choosing to be absent because of poor attitudes or lack of commitment. However, the primary causes were environmental factors that included heavy workloads, longer hours, and insufficient staffing. By focusing only on employee behavior, management risked implementing solutions that would not address the actual problem.

After reviewing the survey results, the organization hired additional staff and redistributed some responsibilities among departments. Within several months, absenteeism decreased and customer satisfaction scores improved. This outcome supports the idea that workplace behavior is often best understood by examining the interaction between the person, motivation, and environment rather than assuming a single cause. In my opinion, managers should focus on diagnosing the root causes of behavior before implementing corrective actions because the most obvious explanation is not always the correct one.

Second and Third Posts Using the 3C + Q Method

I appreciated your discussion of employee turnover following the merger. Your example clearly explained how uncertainty and poor communication influenced employees' decisions to leave the organization. I agree with your argument that compensation was not the primary cause of turnover. Employees often remain with organizations even when higher-paying opportunities exist elsewhere if they feel valued and connected to the organization. This connects to the concept of organizational commitment discussed in this week's materials. The readings explain that employees who identify with organizational goals and feel emotionally attached to the organization are more likely to remain even during difficult periods of change. Do you think turnover would have been reduced if management had communicated more openly about the merger process, or do you think employee departures were unavoidable regardless of communication efforts?

I appreciated your example about the manager who consistently rated one employee as a top performer because of strong communication skills. Your example made it easy to understand how perception can influence managerial decision-making. I agree that managers should be cautious when forming overall impressions based on one highly visible characteristic. Strong presentation skills may create a positive impression, but they do not necessarily indicate high performance in all aspects of a job. This relates to the halo effect discussed in Module 3. The halo effect occurs when one positive characteristic influences a manager's overall evaluation of an employee, potentially causing other strengths and weaknesses to be overlooked. What types of performance evaluation systems do you think organizations could implement to reduce the influence of the halo effect during performance reviews?
