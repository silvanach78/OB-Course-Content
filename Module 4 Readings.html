Motivation and Work Design

Motivation is a central driver of employee behavior and organizational effectiveness. It refers to the desire to achieve a goal or a certain level of performance, leading to goal-directed behavior. However, motivation alone does not guarantee high performance. Consistent with earlier concepts in Organizational Behavior, performance is best understood as a function of ability, motivation, and the environment. Even highly motivated employees will struggle without the necessary skills or organizational support, while capable employees may underperform if they lack motivation.

For managers, the key challenge is not simply to increase effort, but to understand what drives motivation and how it can be influenced. Over time, scholars have developed different theoretical perspectives to explain why individuals exert effort and how organizations can create conditions that sustain motivation.

Motivation theories have evolved from early models focused on universal human needs to more complex frameworks that emphasize cognition, fairness, and the work environment. These perspectives can be grouped into three broad categories: need-based theories, which focus on what motivates individuals; process theories, which explain how motivation occurs; and contemporary and applied approaches, which translate theory into managerial practice.

This module also uses motivation as a diagnostic tool. Some causes of motivation are located in the person, such as personality, attitudes, values, and needs. Others are located in the situation, such as the work environment, available motivators, job design, leadership, job characteristics, and the broader organizational climate. Managers therefore need to look beyond effort alone and ask whether the conditions surrounding employees support or undermine motivation.

Need-Based Theories of Motivation

Need-based theories of motivation, sometimes called content theories, focus on a foundational question: what do people need that drives their behavior? These theories assume that individuals direct effort toward satisfying unmet needs and that motivation is shaped, at least in part, by what employees are trying to obtain from work.1Alderfer, C. P. (1969). An empirical test of a new theory of human needs. Organizational Behavior and Human Performance, 4(2), 142–175.; Baumeister, R. F. (2016). Toward a general theory of motivation: Problems, challenges, opportunities, and the big picture. Motivation and Emotion, 40, 1–10.; Cook, D. A., & Artino, A. R., Jr. (2016). Motivation to learn: An overview of contemporary theories. Medical Education, 50(10), 997–1014.

Although these theories are older and do not capture all the complexity of modern work, they remain useful because they help managers think systematically about why the same reward, task, or work condition may motivate one employee but not another. In practice, need-based theories remind us that motivation is not one-size-fits-all. Employees differ in what they value, what they lack, and what they are seeking from work.2Ryan, R. M., Bradshaw, E., Deci, E. L., Sternberg, R., & Pickren, W. (2019). A history of human motivation theories. In The Cambridge handbook of the intellectual history of psychology (pp. 391–411).

Need-based theories share the assumption that unmet needs energize behavior. They differ, however, in how they define needs, whether they assume a fixed order, and how useful they are for explaining workplace behavior. Four need-based theories are especially influential in Organizational Behavior: Maslow’s hierarchy of needs, Alderfer’s ERG theory, Herzberg’s two-factor theory, and McClelland’s acquired needs theory.3Gawel, J. E. (2019). Herzberg’s theory of motivation and Maslow’s hierarchy of needs. Practical Assessment, Research, and Evaluation, 5(1), 11.; Herzberg, F. (2003). One more time: How do you motivate employees? Harvard Business Review, 81(1), 86–96.

Maslow’s Hierarchy of Needs

Maslow proposed that human needs are arranged in a hierarchy, beginning with physiological and safety needs and progressing to social needs, esteem, and self-actualization. The central idea is that when lower-level needs are unmet, they dominate attention; once they are sufficiently satisfied, higher-order needs become more influential. In a workplace context, this suggests that employees who are worried about pay, job security, or basic safety are less likely to be strongly motivated by growth opportunities or recognition alone.4Maslow, A. H. (1943). A theory of human motivation. Psychological Review, 50, 370–396.; Maslow, A. H. (1954). Motivation and personality. Harper.

Maslow’s model remains influential because it offers an intuitive way to think about employee motivation and helps explain why the same managerial action can have very different effects across employees. However, research has not strongly supported the theory’s strict hierarchy, nor the claim that only one need is dominant at a time. Cross-cultural evidence also suggests that the ordering of needs may differ across contexts. Even so, Maslow’s framework remains useful as a broad reminder that employees may be motivated by different needs depending on their circumstances.5Baumeister, R. F., & Leary, M. R. (1995). The need to belong: Desire for interpersonal attachments as a fundamental human motivation. Psychological Bulletin, 117, 497–529.; Neher, A. (1991). Maslow’s theory of motivation: A critique. Journal of Humanistic Psychology, 31, 89–112.; Rauschenberger, J., Schmitt, N., & Hunter, J. E. (1980). A test of the need hierarchy concept by a Markov model of change in need strength. Administrative Science Quarterly, 25, 654–670.

Alderfer’s ERG Theory

Alderfer revised Maslow’s framework by condensing needs into three categories: existence, relatedness, and growth. Existence includes material and safety concerns, relatedness captures social connection and recognition from others, and growth refers to development, advancement, and personal accomplishment. Unlike Maslow’s theory, ERG theory does not require needs to be satisfied in a fixed sequence and explicitly recognizes that multiple needs may operate at the same time.6Alderfer, C. P. (1969). An empirical test of a new theory of human needs. Organizational Behavior and Human Performance, 4(2), 142–175.

One of ERG theory’s most useful contributions is the frustration-regression principle. When employees are unable to satisfy higher-level needs, they may shift attention back to lower-level ones. For example, an employee blocked from growth opportunities may place greater emphasis on social connection or security. This makes ERG theory especially useful in organizations, where employees often pursue several needs simultaneously and where setbacks in one area can change what becomes motivationally important.7Alderfer, C. P. (1969). An empirical test of a new theory of human needs. Organizational Behavior and Human Performance, 4(2), 142–175.

Herzberg’s Two-Factor Theory

Herzberg approached motivation differently by arguing that the factors that create dissatisfaction are not the same as those that create satisfaction. He called the first group hygiene factors, which include salary, policies, supervision, working conditions, and job security. When these are poor, employees become dissatisfied. However, improving them does not necessarily create strong motivation; it often only removes dissatisfaction. The second group, motivators, includes achievement, recognition, responsibility, advancement, and growth. These factors, which are more intrinsic to the work itself, are what Herzberg argued truly drive satisfaction and motivation.8Herzberg, F. (1965). The motivation to work among Finnish supervisors. Personnel Psychology, 18, 393–402.; Herzberg, F., Mausner, B., & Snyderman, B. (1959). The motivation to work. Wiley.

For managers, this distinction is highly important because it suggests that fixing pay problems or improving policies is necessary but not sufficient. Organizations that want employees to be energized must also provide meaningful work, recognition, and opportunities to grow. Herzberg’s theory has been criticized because some factors, such as pay, can function differently depending on context, and because the original research method has limitations. Still, the theory’s main lesson remains highly practical: reducing dissatisfaction is not the same as creating motivation.9Cummings, L. L., & Elsalmi, A. M. (1968). Empirical research on the bases and correlates of managerial motivation. Psychological Bulletin, 70, 127–144.; House, R. J., & Wigdor, L. A. (1967). Herzberg’s dual-factor theory of job satisfaction and motivation: A review of the evidence and a criticism. Personnel Psychology, 20, 369–389.

McClelland’s Acquired Needs Theory

McClelland’s theory argues that individuals develop learned needs through life experiences, especially the need for achievement, affiliation, and power. Employees high in achievement seek challenge, feedback, and opportunities to excel. Those high in affiliation value relationships, acceptance, and harmony. Those high in power seek influence and the ability to shape outcomes. Rather than assuming the same needs dominate for everyone, this theory emphasizes that motivational differences across individuals are meaningful and consequential at work.10McClelland, D. C. (1961). The achieving society. Van Nostrand.; McClelland, D. C. (1985). How motives, skills, and values determine what people do. American Psychologist, 40(7), 812–825.

This theory is especially useful for managers because it helps explain why employees respond differently to the same situation. High-achievement employees may thrive on stretch goals and performance feedback, whereas employees high in affiliation may be more motivated by collaboration and social support. A strong need for power can be constructive when it is directed toward organizational goals, but it can also create interpersonal problems if it is pursued mainly for status or control. McClelland’s theory is also valuable because it highlights that characteristics that support performance in one role may become liabilities in another. For example, high achievement can be advantageous in individual contributor roles but may make delegation and coaching more difficult in management positions.11McClelland, D. C., & Boyatzis, R. E. (1982). Leadership motive pattern and long-term success in management. Journal of Applied Psychology, 67, 737–743.; McClelland, D. C., & Burnham, D. H. (1976). Power is the great motivator. Harvard Business Review, 25, 159–166.; Wong, M. M., & Csikszentmihalyi, M. (1991). Affiliation motivation and daily experience: Some issues on gender differences. Journal of Personality and Social Psychology, 60, 154–164.

Need-Focused Motivational Strategies

When these theories are considered together, a clear practical conclusion emerges: organizations motivate employees more effectively when they address multiple levels of need rather than relying on a single incentive. At the most basic level, employees need fair pay, safe working conditions, and some degree of security. When these needs are neglected, people are likely to focus their energy on protecting themselves rather than contributing fully to the organization.12Maslow, A. H. (1943). A theory of human motivation. Psychological Review, 50, 370–396.; Alderfer, C. P. (1969). An empirical test of a new theory of human needs. Organizational Behavior and Human Performance, 4(2), 142–175.

Organizations must also create conditions that support belonging and relatedness. Supportive supervision, respectful treatment, collaboration, and recognition matter because employees are motivated not only by material rewards, but also by the quality of their relationships and their sense of connection to others. In today’s workplace, this is especially important in hybrid and remote contexts, where social needs can be more easily overlooked.13Baumeister, R. F., & Leary, M. R. (1995). The need to belong: Desire for interpersonal attachments as a fundamental human motivation. Psychological Bulletin, 117, 497–529.; Wong, M. M., & Csikszentmihalyi, M. (1991). Affiliation motivation and daily experience: Some issues on gender differences. Journal of Personality and Social Psychology, 60, 154–164.

Beyond these foundational needs, organizations should also provide opportunities for growth, challenge, and recognition. Herzberg’s theory is especially helpful here because it reminds managers that eliminating dissatisfaction is not enough. If work is monotonous, offers no autonomy, and provides little opportunity to achieve or grow, employees may be comfortable but not truly motivated. Jobs that offer challenge, meaningful contribution, visible recognition, and room to develop are more likely to sustain higher motivation over time.14Herzberg, F. (2003). One more time: How do you motivate employees? Harvard Business Review, 81(1), 86–96.; Herzberg, F. (1966). Work and the nature of man. World Publishing.

McClelland’s framework adds a more individualized perspective. Employees high in achievement often respond best to challenging goals, feedback, and opportunities to demonstrate competence. Employees high in affiliation are often energized by teamwork, supportive relationships, and social acceptance. Employees high in power may respond to leadership opportunities, influence, and visible responsibility. For managers, this means that effective motivation often requires tailoring opportunities and rewards to the motivational profile of the employee rather than assuming that everyone values the same outcomes.15McClelland, D. C. (1985). How motives, skills, and values determine what people do. American Psychologist, 40(7), 812–825.; Harrell, A. M., & Stahl, M. J. (1981). A behavioral decision theory approach for measuring McClelland’s trichotomy of needs. Journal of Applied Psychology, 66, 242–247.

Managerial implication. Need-focused motivation works best when managers address multiple needs at once: ensuring that employees feel secure, supported, respected, challenged, and able to grow. These theories are most useful not as rigid formulas, but as frameworks for diagnosing what employees may be missing and what kinds of responses are most likely to matter.

Process Theories of Motivation

Process theories of motivation shift attention from what people need to how motivation actually occurs. These theories assume that employees are not simply driven by unmet needs. Instead, they interpret situations, assess fairness, anticipate consequences, and respond to the rewards and punishments they experience. In this sense, motivation is shaped by cognitive processes, perceptions, and learned associations as much as by underlying needs.16Adams, J. S. (1965). Inequity in social exchange. In L. Berkowitz (Ed.), Advances in Experimental Social Psychology (Vol. 2, pp. 267–299). Academic Press.; Vroom, V. H., Porter, L. W., & Lawler, E. E. (2015). Expectancy theories. In Organizational Behavior 1 (pp. 94–113). Routledge.; Skinner, B. F. (1953). Science and human behavior. Free Press.

This perspective is especially useful for managers because it emphasizes that motivation is dynamic and heavily influenced by the organizational environment. Employees ask whether they are being treated fairly, whether effort will lead to performance, whether performance will be rewarded, and whether rewards are meaningful. They also learn from the consequences of their actions. As a result, process theories provide a powerful framework for diagnosing motivational problems and designing systems that support sustained effort and performance.17Cropanzano, R., Bowen, D. E., & Gilliland, S. W. (2007). The management of organizational justice. Academy of Management Perspectives, 21, 34–48.; Cook, C. W. (1980). Guidelines for managing motivation. Business Horizons, 23, 61–69.

Process theories explain motivation as a function of employee perceptions, judgments, and responses to consequences. Four theories are especially influential in Organizational Behavior: Adams’ equity theory, organizational justice theory, expectancy theory, and reinforcement theory. Together, they help explain why fairness, expectations, and consequences can either strengthen or weaken motivation in powerful ways.18Carrell, M. R., & Dittrich, J. E. (1978). Equity theory: The recent literature, methodological considerations, and new directions. Academy of Management Review, 3, 202–210.; Colquitt, J. A., Conlon, D. E., Wesson, M. J., Porter, C. O. L. H., & Ng, K. Y. (2001). Justice at the millennium: A meta-analytic review of 25 years of organizational justice research. Journal of Applied Psychology, 86, 425–445.

Adams’ Equity Theory

Equity theory proposes that employees are motivated by fairness. Individuals compare their own ratio of inputs to outcomes with that of a referent other. Inputs may include effort, experience, education, loyalty, and time, while outcomes may include pay, recognition, status, or opportunities. When employees believe that their ratio is similar to that of a comparison person, they perceive equity. When they perceive inequity, tension is created, and they become motivated to reduce it.19Adams, J. S. (1965). Inequity in social exchange. In L. Berkowitz (Ed.), Advances in Experimental Social Psychology (Vol. 2, pp. 267–299). Academic Press.

The practical importance of the theory lies in how employees respond to perceived unfairness. They may reduce effort, seek increased rewards, change the person to whom they compare themselves, distort perceptions of the situation, or leave the organization altogether. Research also shows that in some cases employees respond to underpayment inequity with theft or other withdrawal behaviors. Because fairness judgments are subjective, managers must recognize that even well-intentioned decisions can be interpreted as inequitable if employees do not understand the basis for differences in outcomes.20Goodman, P. S., & Friedman, A. (1971). An examination of Adams’ theory of inequity. Administrative Science Quarterly, 16, 271–288.; Greenberg, J. (1993). Stealing in the name of justice: Informational and interpersonal moderators of theft reactions to underpayment inequity. Organizational Behavior and Human Decision Processes, 54, 81–103.; Schmidt, D. R., & Marwell, G. (1972). Withdrawal and reward reallocation as responses to inequity. Journal of Experimental Social Psychology, 8, 207–211.

Organizational Justice

Organizational justice extends equity theory by recognizing that employees care not only about the fairness of outcomes, but also about the fairness of procedures and interpersonal treatment. Distributive justice refers to the perceived fairness of outcomes such as pay, promotions, or workloads. Procedural justice concerns the fairness of the processes used to arrive at decisions. Interactional justice refers to the degree to which employees are treated with dignity, respect, and transparency in interpersonal interactions.21Colquitt, J. A., Conlon, D. E., Wesson, M. J., Porter, C. O. L. H., & Ng, K. Y. (2001). Justice at the millennium: A meta-analytic review of 25 years of organizational justice research. Journal of Applied Psychology, 86, 425–445.; Cropanzano, R., Bowen, D. E., & Gilliland, S. W. (2007). The management of organizational justice. Academy of Management Perspectives, 21, 34–48.

This broader view of fairness is important because employees often react strongly when procedures are opaque or when managers communicate decisions disrespectfully, even if the final outcome is acceptable. Research shows that justice perceptions influence commitment, job performance, organizational citizenship behavior, customer satisfaction, retaliation, and even psychological well-being. For managers, the implication is clear: fair outcomes matter, but so do voice, consistency, explanation, and respectful treatment.22Brockner, J., & Wiesenfeld, B. M. (1996). An integrative framework for explaining reactions to decisions: Interactive effects of outcomes and procedures. Psychological Bulletin, 120, 189–208.; Greenberg, J. (1993). Stealing in the name of justice: Informational and interpersonal moderators of theft reactions to underpayment inequity. Organizational Behavior and Human Decision Processes, 54, 81–103.; Cohen-Charash, Y., & Spector, P. E. (2001). The role of justice in organizations: A meta-analysis. Organizational Behavior and Human Decision Processes, 86, 278–321.

At the organizational level, justice perceptions are shaped by the systems managers use to allocate outcomes and apply rules. Reward distribution affects distributive justice because employees compare what they receive with what others receive. Policy enforcement affects procedural justice because employees pay attention to whether rules are applied consistently across people and situations. Performance management affects both fairness and motivation because employees use evaluations, feedback, and promotion decisions to judge whether effort, performance, and outcomes are meaningfully connected.

Expectancy Theory

Expectancy theory explains motivation as a rational decision process. According to this model, motivation depends on three judgments. Expectancy is the belief that effort will lead to successful performance. Instrumentality is the belief that performance will lead to outcomes. Valence is the value the employee places on those outcomes. Motivation is highest when employees believe they can perform successfully, trust that performance will be rewarded, and value the rewards being offered.23Vroom, V. H. (1964). Work and motivation. Wiley.; Porter, L. W., & Lawler, E. E. (1968). Managerial attitudes and performance. Irwin.

This theory is especially powerful because it offers direct managerial guidance. Employees are unlikely to be motivated if they lack the resources or clarity to perform, if reward systems are inconsistent or opaque, or if the available rewards are not meaningful to them. In other words, motivation drops when employees do not believe effort will matter, when they doubt the organization will follow through, or when the outcome simply is not attractive. Managers therefore need to strengthen all three links rather than focusing only on incentives.24Cook, C. W. (1980). Guidelines for managing motivation. Business Horizons, 23, 61–69.; Vroom, V. H., Porter, L. W., & Lawler, E. E. (2015). Expectancy theories. In Organizational Behavior 1 (pp. 94–113). Routledge.

Reinforcement Theory

Reinforcement theory takes a behavioral rather than cognitive approach. It argues that behavior is a function of its consequences. Behaviors followed by favorable outcomes are more likely to be repeated, while behaviors followed by unfavorable outcomes become less likely. This idea builds on Thorndike’s law of effect and the later work of Skinner on operant conditioning.25Thorndike, E. (1927). The law of effect. The American Journal of Psychology, 39(1/4), 212–222.; Skinner, B. F. (1953). Science and human behavior. Free Press.; Skinner, B. F. (1958). Reinforcement today. American Psychologist, 13(3), 94–99.

In organizations, the theory highlights an important managerial reality: employees learn quickly from what is rewarded, ignored, or punished. Desired behaviors that go unrecognized may fade, while undesirable behaviors that accidentally produce positive consequences may become more frequent. This is why reinforcement theory is especially useful in performance management and reward system design. Managers do not need to rely only on formal incentives; everyday responses such as praise, recognition, attention, or silence also shape future behavior.26Stajkovic, A. D., & Luthans, F. (1997). A meta-analysis of the effects of organizational behavior modification on task performance, 1975–1995. Academy of Management Journal, 40(5), 1122–1149.

Process-Focused Motivational Strategies

These theories together suggest that employees are most motivated when they believe the workplace is fair, when they understand how effort leads to outcomes, and when desirable behaviors are reinforced consistently. From an equity perspective, organizations should monitor pay structures, workloads, promotions, and recognition systems so that employees can reasonably perceive them as fair. Transparency matters because employees often fill informational gaps with assumptions, and those assumptions are not always favorable.27Adams, J. S. (1965). Inequity in social exchange. In L. Berkowitz (Ed.), Advances in Experimental Social Psychology (Vol. 2, pp. 267–299). Academic Press.; Carrell, M. R., & Dittrich, J. E. (1978). Equity theory: The recent literature, methodological considerations, and new directions. Academy of Management Review, 3, 202–210.

From an organizational justice perspective, managers should go beyond fair outcomes and ensure that procedures are consistent, employees have voice where appropriate, and decisions are explained respectfully. Employees are more likely to accept even unfavorable outcomes when they believe the process was fair and when they feel treated with dignity. This is especially relevant in performance evaluations, reward decisions, restructuring, and disciplinary situations.28Brockner, J., Fishman, A. Y., Reb, J., Goldman, B., Spiegel, S., & Garden, C. (2007). Procedural fairness, outcome favorability, and judgments of an authority’s responsibility. Journal of Applied Psychology, 92, 1657–1671.; Cropanzano, R., Bowen, D. E., & Gilliland, S. W. (2007). The management of organizational justice. Academy of Management Perspectives, 21, 34–48.

Expectancy theory suggests that managers should clarify role expectations, ensure that employees have the resources and support to succeed, and make performance–reward relationships visible and credible. Employees must be able to see the line of sight between their effort, their performance, and the outcomes that follow. Rewards also need to align with what employees actually value, which requires managers to avoid assuming that everyone is motivated by the same incentives.29Cook, C. W. (1980). Guidelines for managing motivation. Business Horizons, 23, 61–69.; Vroom, V. H., Porter, L. W., & Lawler, E. E. (2015). Expectancy theories. In Organizational Behavior 1 (pp. 94–113). Routledge.

Reinforcement theory adds an important reminder: organizations should pay close attention to the behaviors they are actually reinforcing. If collaboration, initiative, or quality are valued, those behaviors must be noticed and rewarded. If managers inadvertently reward shortcuts, politicking, or visible busyness instead of meaningful results, employees will learn accordingly. In practice, motivational systems are strongest when fairness, clarity, and reinforcement are aligned.30Skinner, B. F. (1953). Science and human behavior. Free Press.; Stajkovic, A. D., & Luthans, F. (1997). A meta-analysis of the effects of organizational behavior modification on task performance, 1975–1995. Academy of Management Journal, 40(5), 1122–1149.

Managerial implication. Process-focused motivation is strongest when organizations build systems employees perceive as fair, understandable, and responsive. Employees are more likely to invest effort when they trust the process, believe effort will matter, and see that desired behaviors are consistently reinforced.

Contemporary Theories of Motivation

Contemporary theories of motivation build on earlier need-based and process approaches, but they place stronger emphasis on intrinsic motivation, autonomy, meaning, and the psychological experience of work. These theories emerged in response to changes in the workplace, especially the growth of knowledge work, team-based work, and jobs that require creativity, judgment, and continuous learning. In these settings, motivation cannot be understood only in terms of external rewards or unmet needs. It also depends on whether employees feel capable, empowered, connected, and psychologically invested in what they do.31Deci, E. L., & Ryan, R. M. (2012). Self-determination theory. In Handbook of theories of social psychology (Vol. 1, pp. 416–433).; Gagné, M., & Deci, E. L. (2005). Self-determination theory and work motivation. Journal of Organizational Behavior, 26(4), 331–362.

These perspectives are especially relevant because many organizations now depend on employees not only to perform assigned tasks, but also to solve problems, collaborate effectively, adapt to change, and contribute ideas. As a result, contemporary theories help managers think beyond simple reward systems and focus on how work can be designed and led in ways that sustain deeper and more durable motivation.32Ahearne, M., Mathieu, J., & Rapp, A. (2005). To empower or not to empower your sales force? An empirical examination of the influence of leadership empowerment behavior on customer satisfaction and performance. Journal of Applied Psychology, 90, 945–955.; Chen, G., Kirkman, B. L., Kanfer, R., Allen, D., & Rosen, B. (2007). A multilevel study of leadership, empowerment, and performance in teams. Journal of Applied Psychology, 92, 331–346.

Contemporary motivational theories are especially concerned with the quality of motivation rather than simply the amount of effort employees exert. They ask whether employees are motivated because they feel controlled and pressured, or because they find their work meaningful, valuable, and engaging. Three ideas are especially influential in this area: self-determination theory, psychological empowerment, and goal setting theory. Together, they help explain why some work environments energize employees while others produce compliance without real commitment.33Gagné, M., & Deci, E. L. (2005). Self-determination theory and work motivation. Journal of Organizational Behavior, 26(4), 331–362.; Thomas, K. W., & Velthouse, B. A. (1990). Cognitive elements of empowerment: An interpretive model of intrinsic task motivation. Academy of Management Review, 15(4), 666–681.; Locke, E. A., & Latham, G. P. (1990). A theory of goal setting and task performance. Prentice Hall.

Self-Determination Theory

Self-determination theory proposes that employees are most likely to experience strong and sustainable motivation when three basic psychological needs are satisfied: autonomy, competence, and relatedness. Autonomy refers to the need to feel that one has meaningful choice or control over one’s actions. Competence reflects the need to feel capable, effective, and able to master challenges. Relatedness refers to the need to feel connected to and valued by others. When these needs are met, employees are more likely to experience intrinsic motivation, meaning that they engage in work because they find it interesting, meaningful, or personally worthwhile rather than simply because they are trying to obtain an external reward or avoid punishment.34Deci, E. L., & Ryan, R. M. (2012). Self-determination theory. In Handbook of theories of social psychology (Vol. 1, pp. 416–433).; Gagné, M., & Deci, E. L. (2005). Self-determination theory and work motivation. Journal of Organizational Behavior, 26(4), 331–362.

This theory is important because it explains why an employee can be externally compliant without being deeply motivated. For example, someone may complete a task because a manager insists, but that is very different from feeling energized by the work itself. Self-determination theory suggests that controlling environments, micromanagement, poor feedback, and weak social connection can undermine intrinsic motivation even when pay is competitive. In contrast, work environments that support discretion, skill development, and positive relationships are more likely to foster stronger engagement and persistence over time.35Deci, E. L., Koestner, R., & Ryan, R. M. (1999). A meta-analytic review of experiments examining the effects of extrinsic rewards on intrinsic motivation. Psychological Bulletin, 125(6), 627–668.; Gagné, M., & Deci, E. L. (2005). Self-determination theory and work motivation. Journal of Organizational Behavior, 26(4), 331–362.

For managers, the theory has direct implications. Employees are more motivated when they understand why a task matters, have some discretion in how to accomplish it, receive developmental feedback that strengthens competence, and feel part of a supportive work group. This does not mean managers should remove all structure. Rather, it means structure should support autonomy and growth rather than crowd them out.

The social context of work also matters. A supportive climate can strengthen autonomy, competence, and relatedness, while a controlling or disrespectful climate can undermine them. As a result, self-determination theory helps explain why two employees with similar skills may show very different levels of motivation depending on the quality of leadership, feedback, resources, and relationships surrounding their work.

Psychological Empowerment

Psychological empowerment focuses on how employees experience their role in the organization and whether that role generates intrinsic task motivation. It consists of four dimensions: meaning, competence, self-determination, and impact. Meaning refers to the degree to which the work feels personally important. Competence refers to confidence in one’s ability to perform well. Self-determination reflects autonomy in how work is carried out. Impact refers to the belief that one’s actions can influence outcomes in the organization. When employees experience these four elements, they are more likely to feel energized, responsible, and committed to their work.36Thomas, K. W., & Velthouse, B. A. (1990). Cognitive elements of empowerment: An interpretive model of intrinsic task motivation. Academy of Management Review, 15(4), 666–681.; Spreitzer, G. M. (1995). Psychological empowerment in the workplace: Dimensions, measurement, and validation. Academy of Management Journal, 38, 1442–1465.

Importantly, psychological empowerment is not just an internal attitude. It is influenced by structural conditions in the workplace. Employees are more likely to feel empowered when they have access to information, resources, support, learning opportunities, and some control over their work. Leadership behavior also matters. Managers who share information, involve employees in decisions, encourage problem solving, and provide developmental support create the structural conditions that make empowerment possible. Without these structural supports, telling employees they are empowered is unlikely to have much effect.37Liden, R. C., Wayne, S. J., & Sparrowe, R. T. (2000). An examination of the mediating role of psychological empowerment on the relations between the job, interpersonal relationships, and work outcomes. Journal of Applied Psychology, 85, 407–416.; Chen, G., Kirkman, B. L., Kanfer, R., Allen, D., & Rosen, B. (2007). A multilevel study of leadership, empowerment, and performance in teams. Journal of Applied Psychology, 92, 331–346.; Ahearne, M., Mathieu, J., & Rapp, A. (2005). To empower or not to empower your sales force? Journal of Applied Psychology, 90, 945–955.

For managers, this means empowerment is both psychological and structural. Employees are unlikely to feel meaning, self-determination, or impact if decisions are centralized, information is withheld, and discretion is tightly constrained. Empowerment therefore requires more than encouraging words. It requires systems, access, trust, and leadership practices that allow employees to exercise judgment in meaningful ways.

Psychological empowerment also helps explain why motivation can influence outcomes beyond the individual employee. Empowered employees are more likely to take initiative, solve problems, and contribute ideas, which can support creativity and organizational innovation. In customer-facing roles, empowerment can also improve responsiveness and service quality, which may strengthen customer satisfaction when employees have the authority and confidence to address customer needs effectively.

Goal Setting Theory

Goal setting theory is one of the most widely used and most strongly supported theories of motivation in both research and practice. It proposes that specific and challenging goals lead to higher performance than vague goals, easy goals, or no goals at all, especially when employees receive feedback and are committed to the goal. Goals motivate by directing attention, increasing effort, encouraging persistence, and prompting employees to develop strategies for performance.38Locke, E. A., & Latham, G. P. (1990). A theory of goal setting and task performance. Prentice Hall.; Locke, E. A., & Latham, G. P. (2002). Building a practically useful theory of goal setting and task motivation: A 35-year odyssey. American Psychologist, 57(9), 705–717.

The theory is highly practical, which helps explain its influence. Managers use goals in performance management systems, project planning, coaching, and development. One widely used application is the idea of SMART goals, which encourages goals to be specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound. SMART goals are not the theory itself, but they translate its core logic into a usable managerial tool by helping managers and employees create goals that are clearer, more observable, and easier to monitor over time.39Locke, E. A., & Latham, G. P. (2002). Building a practically useful theory of goal setting and task motivation: A 35-year odyssey. American Psychologist, 57(9), 705–717.; Drucker, P. F. (1954). The practice of management. Harper & Row.

At the same time, the theory also has boundaries. Goals are most motivating when employees are committed to them, believe they are attainable, and receive feedback that helps them adjust their effort and strategies. Poorly designed goals can create problems. Unrealistic or narrowly defined goals may encourage frustration, tunnel vision, or even unethical behavior if employees become overly focused on hitting a target at any cost. For this reason, managers must treat goals as part of a broader motivational system rather than as a simple performance lever.

Goal setting also operates in group contexts. Many employees pursue individual goals while also contributing to team goals, which means that effective group management and healthy team dynamics influence whether goals motivate or frustrate people. When team goals are clear, roles are coordinated, and feedback is constructive, goal setting can improve both individual effort and group performance. When goals conflict or accountability is unclear, goal setting can create competition, confusion, or disengagement.

Work Design Motivational Strategies

The most practical contributions of motivation research come from approaches that translate theory into workplace design and managerial practices.

Designing Meaningful Jobs

Contemporary motivation research emphasizes that employees are more motivated when the work itself is engaging, meaningful, and aligned with their psychological needs. Self-determination theory explains that intrinsic motivation is strongest when employees experience autonomy, competence, and relatedness in their work. In practice, these elements are often embedded through job design. Jobs that provide meaningful responsibility, opportunities to develop skills, and a clear sense of purpose are more likely to foster sustained motivation than jobs that rely primarily on external rewards.40Gagné, M., & Deci, E. L. (2005). Self-determination theory and work motivation. Journal of Organizational Behavior, 26(4), 331–362.; Deci, E. L., & Ryan, R. M. (2012). Self-determination theory. In Handbook of theories of social psychology (Vol. 1, pp. 416–433).

Similarly, the job characteristics model focuses on desinging meaningful jobs. The model identifies five core job dimensions: skill variety, task identity, task significance, autonomy, and feedback, that influence motivation through their impact on psychological states. Jobs that are meaningful, provide responsibility, and offer feedback are more likely to lead to higher motivation and performance.

This perspective reinforces the idea that motivation is not something that can simply be added through incentives. Instead, it must be built into how work is structured. When employees understand how their work contributes to broader organizational goals, have some discretion in how they perform their tasks, and receive feedback that helps them grow, they are more likely to feel internally motivated and engaged.

Empowerment Through Structure and Leadership

Psychological empowerment highlights that motivation depends not only on the design of work, but also on how employees experience their role within the organization. Employees are more motivated when they perceive their work as meaningful, feel capable of performing effectively, have autonomy in their actions, and believe their efforts have an impact. However, these perceptions do not arise in isolation. They are shaped by the structural and leadership context in which employees operate.41Thomas, K. W., & Velthouse, B. A. (1990). Cognitive elements of empowerment: An interpretive model of intrinsic task motivation. Academy of Management Review, 15(4), 666–681.; Spreitzer, G. M. (1995). Psychological empowerment in the workplace: Dimensions, measurement, and validation. Academy of Management Journal, 38, 1442–1465.

Organizations foster empowerment by providing access to information, resources, and support, as well as by allowing employees discretion in decision-making. Leadership plays a critical role in reinforcing these conditions. Managers who involve employees in decisions, encourage initiative, and provide developmental feedback are more likely to create an environment where empowerment is experienced rather than simply communicated. Without these structural supports, attempts to empower employees may feel superficial and fail to improve motivation.42Liden, R. C., Wayne, S. J., & Sparrowe, R. T. (2000). An examination of the mediating role of psychological empowerment on the relations between the job, interpersonal relationships, and work outcomes. Journal of Applied Psychology, 85, 407–416.; Ahearne, M., Mathieu, J., & Rapp, A. (2005). To empower or not to empower your sales force? Journal of Applied Psychology, 90, 945–955.; Chen, G., Kirkman, B. L., Kanfer, R., Allen, D., & Rosen, B. (2007). A multilevel study of leadership, empowerment, and performance in teams. Journal of Applied Psychology, 92, 331–346.

Goal Setting and Performance Management

Goal setting theory remains one of the most widely used and practically influential approaches to motivation. In organizational settings, it is most visible through performance management systems, where goals are used to direct effort, clarify expectations, and evaluate performance. Research consistently shows that specific and challenging goals, combined with feedback, lead to higher performance than vague or easy goals.43Locke, E. A., & Latham, G. P. (1990). A theory of goal setting and task performance. Prentice Hall.; Locke, E. A., & Latham, G. P. (2002). Building a practically useful theory of goal setting and task motivation: A 35-year odyssey. American Psychologist, 57(9), 705–717.

A widely used application of this theory is the concept of SMART goals, which encourages goals to be specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound. While SMART goals provide a useful framework, their effectiveness depends on proper implementation. Goals must be meaningful, aligned with organizational priorities, and supported by feedback that allows employees to adjust their effort and strategies over time. When used effectively, goal setting provides direction and focus. When used poorly, it can create confusion or unintended consequences.44Locke, E. A., & Latham, G. P. (2002). Building a practically useful theory of goal setting and task motivation: A 35-year odyssey. American Psychologist, 57(9), 705–717.; Drucker, P. F. (1954). The practice of management. Harper & Row.

Gamification and Modern Motivation Tools

Some organizations have introduced gamification as a way to enhance motivation by incorporating elements such as points, rankings, and rewards into work processes. These tools can increase engagement in the short term, particularly when tasks are repetitive or lack inherent interest. However, their effectiveness depends on how they are designed and implemented.45Deterding, S., Dixon, D., Khaled, R., & Nacke, L. (2011). From game design elements to gamefulness: Defining gamification. In Proceedings of the 15th International Academic MindTrek Conference (pp. 9–15). ACM.; Hamari, J., Koivisto, J., & Sarsa, H. (2014). Does gamification work? A literature review of empirical studies. In 2014 47th Hawaii International Conference on System Sciences (pp. 3025–3034). IEEE.

Gamification is most effective when it supports intrinsic motivation by making work more engaging or providing meaningful feedback. It is less effective when it relies solely on external rewards, which may shift attention away from the work itself. In such cases, employees may become more focused on earning rewards than on performing well or contributing meaningfully to organizational goals.

Risks and Misalignment in Motivation Systems

Although contemporary motivational strategies provide powerful tools, they can also be misapplied. Poorly designed systems may undermine motivation rather than enhance it. For example, overly aggressive or narrowly defined goals can encourage unethical behavior, reduce collaboration, or create excessive pressure. Similarly, empowerment without sufficient structure, resources, or clarity can lead to role ambiguity and stress instead of increased motivation.46Locke, E. A., & Latham, G. P. (2002). Building a practically useful theory of goal setting and task motivation: A 35-year odyssey. American Psychologist, 57(9), 705–717.; Chen, G., Kirkman, B. L., Kanfer, R., Allen, D., & Rosen, B. (2007). A multilevel study of leadership, empowerment, and performance in teams. Journal of Applied Psychology, 92, 331–346.

Efforts to enhance intrinsic motivation may also fail if basic organizational systems are not functioning effectively. Employees are unlikely to feel motivated if rewards are perceived as unfair, expectations are unclear, or leadership is inconsistent. In practice, motivation depends on alignment across systems. Work design, leadership behavior, and performance management must reinforce one another rather than operate in isolation.

Contemporary and Work Design Motivation Strategies

Taken together, contemporary theories suggest that modern motivation is strongest when organizations create conditions that support meaningful, self-directed, and competent action. Employees are more likely to invest sustained effort when they find their work worthwhile, feel capable of succeeding, and experience enough autonomy to exercise judgment rather than simply comply with instructions. These theories therefore direct managers’ attention away from rewards alone and toward the broader psychological and structural environment of work.47Gagné, M., & Deci, E. L. (2005). Self-determination theory and work motivation. Journal of Organizational Behavior, 26(4), 331–362.; Spreitzer, G. M. (1995). Psychological empowerment in the workplace: Dimensions, measurement, and validation. Academy of Management Journal, 38, 1442–1465.

In practice, this means motivation should be supported at several levels simultaneously. Work should be designed to provide meaning and growth, leadership should reinforce autonomy and confidence, performance systems should clarify goals and provide useful feedback, and newer tools such as gamification should be used carefully so that they enhance engagement rather than crowd out intrinsic interest. The theories are most powerful when they are applied as a coordinated system rather than as isolated techniques.48Locke, E. A., & Latham, G. P. (2002). Building a practically useful theory of goal setting and task motivation: A 35-year odyssey. American Psychologist, 57(9), 705–717.; Ahearne, M., Mathieu, J., & Rapp, A. (2005). To empower or not to empower your sales force? Journal of Applied Psychology, 90, 945–955.; Hamari, J., Koivisto, J., & Sarsa, H. (2014). Does gamification work? A literature review of empirical studies. In 2014 47th Hawaii International Conference on System Sciences (pp. 3025–3034). IEEE.

Job design has important consequences for workplace attitudes, well-being, and turnover. When jobs provide meaningful work, autonomy, feedback, and opportunities for skill use, employees are more likely to feel engaged and committed. When jobs are poorly designed, overly controlled, or disconnected from meaningful outcomes, employees may become dissatisfied, withdraw effort, or eventually leave the organization.

Managerial implication. For managers, the broader lesson is that contemporary motivation is not just about encouraging more effort. It is about creating a work environment in which employees can experience ownership, progress, and purpose. When these elements are present, motivation is more likely to be durable, ethical, and aligned with high performance.

Key Takeaways

Motivation should be understood as a multilevel issue. At the individual level, motivation affects task performance, workplace attitudes, well-being, turnover, and creativity. At the group level, it affects coordination, effort, and group performance. At the organizational level, these patterns can influence financial performance, customer satisfaction, innovation, and reputation. Employees who experience unfairness, weak leadership, poor job design, or demotivating work conditions often share those experiences with others, which can affect the organization’s image as an employer and service provider.

  • Motivation is a key driver of performance but must be considered alongside ability and environmental factors.
  • Need-based theories explain what motivates individuals but are limited in their ability to predict behavior.
  • Process theories emphasize that motivation depends on expectations, fairness, and perceived outcomes.
  • Expectancy theory highlights the importance of aligning effort, performance, and rewards.
  • Perceptions of fairness strongly influence motivation, behavior, and organizational outcomes.
  • Intrinsic motivation is driven by autonomy, competence, and meaningful work.
  • Job design, goal setting, and performance management are critical tools for enhancing motivation.
  • Managers play a central role in shaping motivation by influencing both the work environment and employee perceptions.

Managerial Insight

Motivation is one of the most powerful levers managers have for influencing performance and organizational outcomes. While managers cannot change all aspects of employees’ abilities or personal characteristics, they have significant control over the work environment, reward systems, and leadership practices that shape motivation.

Effective managers recognize that motivation is not a one-size-fits-all process. Employees differ in what they value, how they interpret fairness, and how they respond to incentives. As a result, motivation strategies must be tailored to align with individual needs, perceptions, and goals.

Perhaps most importantly, managers must ensure alignment between effort, performance, and rewards. When employees believe that their effort will lead to performance, that performance will be recognized, and that rewards are meaningful, they are more likely to invest discretionary effort. When this alignment breaks down, motivation declines, regardless of how capable employees may be.

Ultimately, motivation is not about forcing employees to work harder. It is about creating conditions where employees choose to perform at their best. This requires thoughtful job design, fair and transparent systems, and a work environment that supports both performance and well-being.
