Decision-Making and Managing Stakeholder Trade-Offs

Introduction

Decision-making is one of the central activities of management. Managers decide how to allocate resources, respond to problems, evaluate alternatives, communicate with stakeholders, and act when outcomes are uncertain. These decisions may look technical on the surface, but they are also behavioral and ethical because they are shaped by judgment, emotion, bias, group dynamics, power, culture, and competing stakeholder interests. A manager who understands decision-making is better prepared to diagnose problems, evaluate alternatives carefully, and explain recommendations in a way that is credible to decision makers.

This module connects decision-making with stakeholder analysis because your consulting project requires both. In that project, you will advise a specific decision maker on a current ethics-related organizational problem, identify stakeholders, evaluate three alternative courses of action, discuss risks and risk mitigation, and recommend one course of action. The goal is not simply to describe what happened in the news. The goal is to think like a consultant: define the problem clearly, use ethical and OB frameworks, identify who is affected, compare realistic alternatives, and justify a recommendation that the decision maker can actually implement.20Chambers, S. (2026). Consulting Project: Assignment Instructions. Course material.

Conflict management is central to stakeholder analysis. Stakeholders often disagree because they have different goals, risks, resources, values, and definitions of fairness. Strong recommendations should therefore explain not only what the decision maker should do, but also how predictable stakeholder conflict should be handled.

Instructor's note. This module will help you complete your consulting project; after completing it, you should be able to distinguish decision types, compare decision-making models, identify biases and fallacies, explain when groups improve or harm decision quality, and conduct a stakeholder analysis that supports a defensible recommendation.

Understanding Managerial Decision-Making

Decision-making is the process of choosing among alternative courses of action, including the choice to take no action. This definition is important because inaction is still a choice. A manager who ignores a workplace safety concern, delays responding to a public criticism, or avoids disciplining a high-performing employee who behaves unethically has made a decision, even if no formal action was taken. Because managerial decisions can affect employees, customers, shareholders, communities, suppliers, regulators, and other groups, decision quality matters for both organizational effectiveness and ethical responsibility.1Ireland, R. D., & Miller, C. C. (2004). Decision making and firm success. Academy of Management Executive, 18, 8–12; Nutt, P. C. (1998). Surprising but true: Half the decisions in organizations fail. Academy of Management Executive, 13, 75–90; Nutt, P. C. (2002). Why decisions fail. Berrett-Koehler; Barends, E., Rousseau, D. M., & Briner, R. B. (2015). Evidence-Based Management: How to Use Evidence to Make Better Organizational Decisions. Kogan Page.

One reason decision-making deserves careful attention is that many managerial decisions fail to achieve their intended outcomes. Decisions can fail because managers define the problem poorly, search too narrowly for alternatives, rely on incomplete or biased information, allow politics or group pressure to dominate the process, or ignore stakeholders whose reactions later affect implementation. This is why evidence-based management is not only about collecting data. It is about asking better questions, using relevant evidence, considering context, and recognizing the limits of personal judgment.1Ireland, R. D., & Miller, C. C. (2004). Decision making and firm success. Academy of Management Executive, 18, 8–12; Nutt, P. C. (1998). Surprising but true: Half the decisions in organizations fail. Academy of Management Executive, 13, 75–90; Nutt, P. C. (2002). Why decisions fail. Berrett-Koehler; Barends, E., Rousseau, D. M., & Briner, R. B. (2015). Evidence-Based Management: How to Use Evidence to Make Better Organizational Decisions. Kogan Page.

Managerial decisions vary in scope. Strategic decisions set the overall direction of an organization and are usually made by top leaders or boards. Tactical decisions translate strategy into policies, programs, or departmental actions. Operational decisions occur in daily work and keep the organization functioning. For example, if a restaurant chain wants to improve customer satisfaction, executives may make a strategic decision to compete on service quality. District managers may make a tactical decision to create a customer complaint policy, while servers make operational decisions about how to respond to specific customer concerns. Each level matters because small operational decisions can either support or undermine the organization’s broader strategy.

Types of Decisions

Decision-making can be classified in two complementary ways. The first classification focuses on how the brain processes information. The second focuses on the structure of the decision itself. Students often confuse these frameworks because both compare fast and slow decision-making, but they answer different questions. Reflective and reactive systems describe how people think; programmed and nonprogrammed decisions describe the type of decision situation managers face.

Table 5-1. Two Ways to Classify Decisions
FrameworkCategoriesWhat It DescribesManagerial Example
Cognitive processingReflective vs. reactive systemsHow the brain processes information when making a choice.A manager carefully analyzing market entry risks uses reflective processing; an experienced supervisor quickly responding to an equipment failure may use reactive processing.
Decision structureProgrammed vs. nonprogrammed decisionsWhether the decision is routine and rule-based or novel and unstructured.Approving routine expense reports is programmed; responding to a major ethics crisis is nonprogrammed.

The reactive system, often associated with System 1 thinking, is fast, intuitive, automatic, and emotionally influenced. It is useful when a decision must be made quickly or when the decision maker has enough experience to recognize patterns accurately. However, reactive processing is also more vulnerable to bias because it relies on mental shortcuts. The reflective system, often associated with System 2 thinking, is slower, deliberate, analytical, and effortful. It is better suited for novel, complex, high-stakes decisions that require careful comparison of alternatives.2Kahneman, D. (2011). Thinking, Fast and Slow. Farrar, Straus and Giroux; Tversky, A., & Kahneman, D. (1974). Judgment under uncertainty: Heuristics and biases. Science, 185(4157), 1124–1131.

Programmed decisions are routine and repetitive. Because they occur frequently, organizations can use rules, policies, standard operating procedures, or decision trees to guide action. Nonprogrammed decisions are novel, ambiguous, or high-stakes. They require judgment because there is no existing rule that fully resolves the problem. For your consulting project, the problem you select should be nonprogrammed. If the organization could solve the issue simply by following an existing policy, there would be little need for a consulting analysis.

These frameworks overlap but are not identical. Programmed decisions often rely on reactive processing because routine situations allow managers to act quickly. Nonprogrammed decisions usually require reflective processing because the manager must define the problem, generate alternatives, weigh risks, and consider stakeholders. Expertise can blur the distinction. An experienced crisis manager may react quickly to early warning signs, but that speed is based on years of learning, not guessing.

Decision-Making Models

Decision-making models help managers understand how decisions should be made, how they are actually made, and how they can be improved. No single model fits every situation. A routine issue may not require a full formal analysis, but a complex ethical problem involving multiple stakeholders should not be handled by intuition alone. Four models are especially useful for understanding managerial decision-making: the rational model, bounded rationality, intuitive decision-making, and creative decision-making.

The Rational Decision-Making Model

The rational decision-making model describes a structured process for making high-quality decisions. The model assumes that decision makers can define the problem clearly, identify criteria, weigh those criteria, generate alternatives, evaluate each alternative, choose the best option, implement it, and evaluate the outcome. This model is most useful when the decision is important, enough time is available, relevant information can be gathered, and the consequences of a poor decision are significant.6Nutt, P. C. (1994). Types of organizational decision processes. Administrative Science Quarterly, 29, 414–550; Nutt, P. C. (1998). Surprising but true: Half the decisions in organizations fail. Academy of Management Executive, 13, 75–90.

Table 5-2. Steps in the Rational Decision-Making Model
StepWhat the Decision Maker DoesApplication to the Consulting Project

2. Establish criteriaDetermine what standards should guide the decision.Use ethical frameworks, stakeholder impacts, feasibility, risk, and alignment with organizational goals.
3. Weigh criteriaDecide which criteria matter most.Explain why some concerns, such as safety, fairness, legality, or trust, may outweigh short-term cost.
4. Generate alternativesCreate multiple possible courses of action.Develop at least three realistic alternatives.

The rational model is valuable because it encourages managers to define criteria before falling in love with a preferred option. For example, a manager responding to public criticism might immediately prefer a public apology because it seems fast and visible. However, if the decision criteria include fairness to affected employees, legal exposure, long-term trust, and operational feasibility, the manager may realize that an apology without policy change is insufficient. The model also reminds students to generate alternatives. Research on organizational decisions shows that failure often occurs because decision makers do not search broadly enough for alternatives.6Nutt, P. C. (1994). Types of organizational decision processes. Administrative Science Quarterly, 29, 414–550; Nutt, P. C. (1998). Surprising but true: Half the decisions in organizations fail. Academy of Management Executive, 13, 75–90.

Bounded Rationality and Satisficing

The rational model is useful, but it rests on unrealistic assumptions. In real organizations, managers rarely have complete information, unlimited time, perfect objectivity, or the ability to compare every possible option. Bounded rationality recognizes that people make decisions under cognitive and situational limits. Rather than maximizing, they often satisfice, which means selecting the first alternative that meets minimum acceptable criteria.3Simon, H. A. (1957). Administrative Behavior: A Study of Decision-Making Processes in Administrative Organizations (2nd ed.). Free Press.

Satisficing is not always irresponsible. A manager facing an urgent staffing issue may need a workable solution today rather than the theoretically perfect solution next month. The danger arises when managers satisfice too early on decisions that require deeper analysis. In the consulting project, choosing the first obvious recommendation is usually a sign of weak decision-making. Stronger analysis comes from deliberately developing and comparing multiple alternatives before selecting one.

The Intuitive Decision-Making Model

Intuitive decision-making involves arriving at a decision without conscious step-by-step reasoning. This does not mean the decision is random. In many professional settings, intuition is pattern recognition developed through experience. Fire chiefs, nurses, pilots, and experienced managers often notice cues that novices miss. They may mentally simulate whether a possible action will work, reject it if it seems flawed, and move to another option until they identify a workable course of action.4Dane, E., & Pratt, M. G. (2007). Exploring intuition and its role in managerial decision making. Academy of Management Review, 32(1), 33–54; Klein, G. (2003). Intuition at work. Doubleday; Salas, E., & Klein, G. (2001). Linking expertise and naturalistic decision making. Lawrence Erlbaum Associates.

Intuition is most useful when the decision maker has relevant expertise, the environment provides reliable feedback, and the situation resembles patterns the decision maker has encountered before. It is less reliable when the problem is novel, emotionally charged, politically sensitive, or ethically complex. A manager may have a strong feeling that an employee complaint is exaggerated, but if that feeling is shaped by liking bias or similar-to-me bias, intuition can lead to unfair treatment. For high-stakes decisions, intuition can be an input, but it should be checked against evidence and stakeholder analysis.

The Creative Decision-Making Model

Creative decision-making is important when existing options are inadequate. Creativity involves generating new and useful ideas, while innovation requires implementing those ideas successfully. Creative decision-making is especially relevant when managers face problems that cannot be solved by applying a standard rule or copying what competitors have done. The creative process typically includes problem identification, immersion, incubation, illumination, and verification.5Amabile, T. M. (1988). A model of creativity and innovation in organizations. In B. M. Staw & L. L. Cummings (Eds.), Research in Organizational Behavior, 10, 123–167; Amabile, T. M., Conti, R., Coon, H., Lazenby, J., & Herron, M. (1996). Assessing the work environment for creativity. Academy of Management Journal, 39, 1154–1184; Woodman, R. W., Sawyer, J. E., & Griffin, R. W. (1993). Toward a theory of organizational creativity. Academy of Management Review, 18, 293–321.

In the immersion stage, the decision maker studies the problem deeply, gathers information, and develops expertise. During incubation, the person steps away from the problem while the mind continues working on it in the background. Illumination is the moment when a possible solution becomes clear. Verification involves testing whether the idea is realistic and useful. In your consulting report, creativity may be necessary when the obvious alternatives are too extreme, too costly, or too ethically weak. For example, if a company is accused of harmful monitoring practices, one alternative might be to stop monitoring entirely and another might be to continue unchanged. A more creative alternative may redesign monitoring around transparency, consent, privacy safeguards, and employee voice.

Creativity can be evaluated through fluency, the number of ideas generated; flexibility, the variety of ideas; and originality, the uniqueness of ideas. Brainstorming can help generate ideas, but only if criticism is delayed and participants are encouraged to build on one another’s suggestions. Quantity matters because more ideas increase the likelihood that at least one strong option will emerge.5Amabile, T. M. (1988). A model of creativity and innovation in organizations. In B. M. Staw & L. L. Cummings (Eds.), Research in Organizational Behavior, 10, 123–167; Amabile, T. M., Conti, R., Coon, H., Lazenby, J., & Herron, M. (1996). Assessing the work environment for creativity. Academy of Management Journal, 39, 1154–1184; Woodman, R. W., Sawyer, J. E., & Griffin, R. W. (1993). Toward a theory of organizational creativity. Academy of Management Review, 18, 293–321.

Faulty Decision-Making

Even well-intentioned managers make poor decisions because human judgment is imperfect. Biases, heuristics, emotions, time pressure, uncertainty, and flawed reasoning can distort how people interpret information and evaluate alternatives. These problems matter in OB because managers make decisions about hiring, promotion, discipline, performance evaluation, rewards, conflict, change, and ethics. A biased decision may harm employees directly, but it can also damage trust, culture, and organizational legitimacy.2Kahneman, D. (2011). Thinking, Fast and Slow. Farrar, Straus and Giroux; Tversky, A., & Kahneman, D. (1974). Judgment under uncertainty: Heuristics and biases. Science, 185(4157), 1124–1131.

Logical Fallacies in Managerial Reasoning

Biases influence how information is perceived; logical fallacies weaken how arguments are constructed. Managers need to recognize fallacies because poor reasoning can make weak recommendations sound persuasive. In a consulting report, fallacies are especially dangerous because they can lead students to recommend actions that are emotionally appealing but not logically supported.

Table 5-4. Logical Fallacies to Avoid in Decision Recommendations
FallacyMeaningWeak Managerial ReasoningBetter Reasoning
Non sequiturThe conclusion does not logically follow from the evidence.“The founder has an MBA, so the strategy will work.”Evaluate the strategy using market, financial, ethical, and stakeholder evidence.
False causeAssuming that because two events occurred together, one caused the other.“Turnover fell after free snacks were added, so snacks caused retention.”Consider other explanations such as pay, leadership changes, or labor market conditions.
Ad hominemAttacking the person instead of the argument.“We should ignore the complaint because the employee is new.”Evaluate whether the complaint is supported by evidence.
Genetic fallacyRejecting an idea because of its source.“The idea came from an intern, so it cannot be useful.”Assess the idea on its merits.
Appeal to traditionAssuming the old way is best because it is familiar.“We have always required employees to be onsite, so remote work cannot work.”Compare evidence about performance, coordination, fairness, and role requirements.
BandwagonAssuming an option is good because many others are doing it.“Competitors use AI hiring tools, so we should too.”Evaluate validity, bias risk, privacy, legal exposure, and stakeholder impact.
Appeal to emotionUsing emotion instead of evidence to justify a conclusion.“We must approve this because employees will be upset otherwise.”Consider employee impact, financial constraints, fairness, and alternatives.
Red herringDiverting attention from the main issue.“Instead of discussing safety complaints, let’s talk about employee morale.”Address safety directly while recognizing related morale concerns.
StrawmanMisrepresenting an argument so it is easier to attack.“You support flexible work, so you want no accountability.”Evaluate the actual proposal and its controls.
Tu quoqueDeflecting criticism by accusing the other side of hypocrisy.“Employees also fail to communicate, so leadership does not need to improve communication.”Assess whether leadership communication is effective regardless of employee behavior.

Fallacies are common in organizational debates because managers often make decisions under pressure and may be motivated to defend their preferences. The solution is not to remove judgment from management. The solution is to discipline judgment by asking whether claims follow logically from evidence, whether alternative explanations have been considered, and whether the recommendation fits the actual decision maker’s authority.20Tindale, C. W. (2007). Fallacies and argument appraisal. Cambridge University Press.; Jin, Z., Lalwani, A., Vaidhya, T., Shen, X., Ding, Y., Lyu, Z., ... & Schoelkopf, B. (2022, December). Logical fallacy detection. In Findings of the Association for Computational Linguistics: EMNLP 2022 (pp. 7180-7198).

Decision-Making in Groups

Some decisions are better made by individuals, while others benefit from group decision-making. Groups can bring more knowledge, generate more alternatives, detect weaknesses in proposals, and increase commitment to implementation. This is especially true when the decision requires information held by different people or when implementation depends on the support of those affected. Diverse groups that debate issues can make more comprehensive decisions because members contribute different perspectives and challenge incomplete assumptions.8Simons, T., Pelled, L. H., & Smith, K. A. (1999). Making use of difference: Diversity, debate, decision comprehensiveness in top management teams. Academy of Management Journal, 42, 662–673; Miner, F. C. (1984). Group versus individual decision making. Organizational Behavior and Human Performance, 33, 112–124.

However, while group decision-making can improve decisions by bringing together multiple perspectives, but it also creates challenges when group members have different priorities, incentives, or definitions of fairness. In many organizations, people enter group decisions representing different departments, roles, or stakeholder interests. For example, calibration meetings for merit-based raises often take a long time because each manager may advocate for employees in their own unit, interpret performance scores differently, or define “fairness” in different ways. Budget meetings create similar tensions because managers may agree on the overall goal of supporting the organization but disagree about whether funds should go toward staffing, technology, marketing, training, or operations. Hospitals provide another useful example: when a grant or donation becomes available, each department or specialty may believe the money should be spent on its own priorities because each sees patient care through a different professional lens. These situations show that group decisions are not only cognitive tasks; they are also political and ethical tasks that require managers to balance evidence, competing needs, stakeholder impact, and organizational priorities.

Groups also create other risks. They take more time, require coordination, and can suffer from process losses. The group may defer to a dominant member, avoid disagreement, or spend too much time managing relationships instead of evaluating evidence. Groups rarely outperform their strongest individual member if group processes are poor. Therefore, the managerial question is not simply whether to use a group. The question is how to structure group decision-making so that it improves judgment rather than magnifying bias.

Groupthink

Groupthink occurs when a group prioritizes harmony, conformity, or loyalty over critical evaluation. The group may appear unified, but the agreement may be superficial because members suppress doubts or avoid raising concerns. Groupthink is particularly risky when the group is cohesive, insulated from outside information, led by a directive leader, or under pressure to make an important decision quickly.9Janis, I. L. (1972). Victims of Groupthink. Houghton Mifflin.

Janis identified several symptoms of groupthink, including an illusion of invulnerability, collective rationalizations, unquestioned belief in the group’s morality, stereotyped views of outsiders, direct pressure on dissenters, self-censorship, illusion of unanimity, and self-appointed mindguards who shield the group from contrary information. In practical terms, groupthink occurs when people stop asking, “Is this the right decision?” and start asking, “How do we protect agreement?” A manager can reduce groupthink by inviting dissent, assigning a devil’s advocate, seeking outside input, separating idea generation from evaluation, and ensuring that lower-status members can speak without fear.

Additionally, effective group decision-making requires clear criteria before alternatives are debated. If the group first agrees on decision criteria, such as performance evidence, equity, strategic alignment, urgency, stakeholder impact, and long-term consequences, the discussion is less likely to become a contest of preferences. This is also why stakeholder analysis is valuable: it helps decision-makers identify whose interests are affected, how strongly they are affected, and how much influence they have before choosing a course of action.

Tools and Techniques for Better Group Decisions

Several tools can improve group decision-making by making participation more balanced and information sharing more systematic. The Nominal Group Technique begins with members silently generating ideas, then sharing them in turn, discussing them, and voting or ranking options. This structure helps prevent dominant personalities from controlling the conversation and helps quieter members contribute. The Delphi Technique gathers written responses from experts through multiple rounds of questionnaires. It is useful when participants are geographically dispersed, when anonymity improves candor, or when the goal is to build expert consensus without bringing people into one meeting.10Delbecq, A. L., Van de Ven, A. H., & Gustafson, D. H. (1975). Group techniques for program planning: A guide to nominal group and Delphi processes. Scott Foresman; Lam, S. S. K., & Schaubroeck, J. (2000). Improving group decisions by better pooling information. Journal of Applied Psychology, 85, 565–573; Nunamaker, J. F., Jr., Dennis, A. R., Valacich, J. S., Vogel, D. R., & George, J. F. (1991). Electronic meetings to support group work. Communications of the ACM, 34(7), 40–61.

Majority rule is simple and efficient, but it may reduce commitment among those who opposed the final option. Consensus takes longer but can improve commitment when implementation depends on broad support. Group Decision Support Systems use technology to collect, organize, and share ideas. These systems can improve information sharing, but they can also create overload if too much information is generated without a clear structure for evaluation.10Delbecq, A. L., Van de Ven, A. H., & Gustafson, D. H. (1975). Group techniques for program planning: A guide to nominal group and Delphi processes. Scott Foresman; Lam, S. S. K., & Schaubroeck, J. (2000). Improving group decisions by better pooling information. Journal of Applied Psychology, 85, 565–573; Nunamaker, J. F., Jr., Dennis, A. R., Valacich, J. S., Vogel, D. R., & George, J. F. (1991). Electronic meetings to support group work. Communications of the ACM, 34(7), 40–61.

Decision trees can also help groups by guiding members through a sequence of yes-or-no questions. They are especially useful when an organization wants consistency. For example, a decision tree can help managers decide when to escalate an employee complaint, when to disclose a conflict of interest, or when a customer refund should be issued. The benefit is that similar cases receive similar treatment, which supports fairness and reduces framing bias.

This is why group decision-making and conflict management should be studied together. Groups improve decisions when members can disagree productively, but they harm decisions when disagreement becomes personal, suppressed, or politically driven. The next section explains how managers can diagnose and handle those conflicts before moving into stakeholder analysis.

Ethics and National Culture in Decision-Making

Many managerial decisions have an ethical component because they affect stakeholders and involve questions of fairness, rights, harm, transparency, and responsibility. A decision can be legal but still ethically weak. For example, a company may legally use confusing contract language, aggressive sales tactics, or intrusive monitoring, but legality alone does not determine whether the decision is fair or responsible. Ethical decision-making requires managers to ask not only “Can we do this?” but also “Should we do this?”11Blanchard, K., & Peale, N. V. (1988). The Power of Ethical Management. William Morrow; Manning, J. (2008, March 27). Chase mortgage memo pushes ‘Cheats & Tricks.’ The Oregonian.

Managers can begin with a practical ethical screen. Does the decision break any law? Does it violate organizational rules? Is it fair to those affected? Would the decision maker be comfortable explaining the decision publicly? Does it maximize short-term benefits while shifting costs to employees, customers, communities, or future stakeholders? Would not taking action be fair? These questions do not replace the ethical frameworks from earlier in the course, but they help managers identify when a decision requires deeper ethical analysis.

National culture also shapes decision-making. Some cultures value quick decisions and individual accountability, while others emphasize consensus, relationship building, and careful consultation. Japanese nemawashi, for example, involves building support before a formal decision is made. This approach may slow the front end of the decision process, but it can improve implementation because key stakeholders have already been consulted and concerns have been addressed. Managers working across cultures should avoid assuming that their preferred decision style is universally superior. They should ask whether speed, consensus, hierarchy, participation, or relationship preservation matters most in the context.12Noorderhaven, N. G. (2007). Comprehensiveness versus pragmatism: Consensus at the Japanese-Dutch interface. Journal of Management Studies, 44, 1349–1370.

Understanding Conflict

Conflict is a process that involves disagreement between people, groups, departments, or organizations. In this module, conflict matters because stakeholder analysis is rarely neutral. Stakeholders often want different outcomes, disagree about priorities, or compete for limited resources. A manager who identifies stakeholders but does not anticipate conflict may develop a recommendation that looks reasonable on paper but fails during implementation.21Wall, J. A., & Callister, R. R. (1995). Conflict and its management. Journal of Management, 21, 515–558.; Bergman, T. J., & Volkema, R. J. (1989). Understanding and managing interpersonal conflict at work: Its issues, interactive processes and consequences. In D. M. Kolb & J. M. Kolb (Eds.), Hidden conflict in organizations (pp. 7–19). Sage.

Conflict is not always harmful. Functional conflict supports the goals of the team or organization by encouraging open discussion, surfacing problems, and improving learning. Dysfunctional conflict damages relationships, slows progress, and often reflects personal tension, unmanaged emotion, or poor communication. The practical question is not whether managers should eliminate all conflict. The question is whether they can keep conflict focused on ideas, evidence, priorities, and trade-offs rather than allowing it to become personal or destructive.22Amason, A. C. (1996). Distinguishing the effects of functional and dysfunctional conflict on strategic decision making: Resolving a paradox for top management teams. Academy of Management Journal, 39, 123–148.; Jehn, K. A., & Mannix, E. A. (2001). The dynamic nature of conflict: A longitudinal study of intergroup conflict and group performance. Academy of Management Journal, 44, 238–251.; De Wit, F. R., Greer, L. L., & Jehn, K. A. (2012). The paradox of intragroup conflict: A meta-analysis. Journal of Applied Psychology, 97(2), 360–390.

Conflict often follows an inverted-U relationship with performance. Too little conflict can produce complacency, weak critical evaluation, and groupthink. Too much conflict can overwhelm people, damage trust, and reduce productivity. The goal is the middle range sometimes described as the Goldilocks Zone: enough disagreement to improve thinking, but not so much that the group loses focus, trust, or commitment.22Amason, A. C. (1996). Distinguishing the effects of functional and dysfunctional conflict on strategic decision making: Resolving a paradox for top management teams. Academy of Management Journal, 39, 123–148.; Jehn, K. A., & Mannix, E. A. (2001). The dynamic nature of conflict: A longitudinal study of intergroup conflict and group performance. Academy of Management Journal, 44, 238–251.; De Wit, F. R., Greer, L. L., & Jehn, K. A. (2012). The paradox of intragroup conflict: A meta-analysis. Journal of Applied Psychology, 97(2), 360–390.

For stakeholder analysis, this insight is critical. A recommendation should not simply suppress disagreement. If stakeholders disagree about safety, privacy, layoffs, resource allocation, or product harms, the disagreement may reveal important ethical and operational risks. The manager’s task is to structure the conflict so that different perspectives improve the decision rather than derail it.

Conflict management refers to the strategies and processes used to address disagreement in a productive way. Because conflict varies in source, intensity, and importance, no single conflict-handling style is always best. Managers should choose a style based on the stakes of the issue, the importance of the relationship, the amount of time available, the ethical implications, and the likely effect on stakeholders.28Thomas, K. A widely used way to understand conflict-handling styles compares two dimensions: concern for self, which reflects assertiveness, and concern for others, which reflects cooperativeness. For stakeholder analysis, collaboration is often the most desirable style because it encourages the decision maker to understand underlying interests rather than simply choosing winners and losers. However, collaboration is not always possible. A manager may need to compete when safety, discrimination, harassment, legal compliance, or serious ethical harm is involved. A manager may compromise when resources are limited and all groups have legitimate claims. A manager may avoid temporarily when more information is needed, but avoidance should not become a way to ignore harm. A manager may accommodate when preserving trust or repairing a relationship matters more than winning a low-stakes issue.

Strong consulting recommendations should therefore include a conflict-management logic. Students should explain which stakeholders are likely to disagree, why the conflict exists, which style or process should be used to manage the disagreement, and how the recommended approach protects decision quality, fairness, trust, and implementation.

The Art of War: Lessons for Managing Organizational Conflict

Stakeholder Analysis

Stakeholder analysis is also a conflict-diagnosis tool. Stakeholders often disagree because they have different goals, different levels of power, different exposure to risk, and different definitions of fairness. For this reason, a strong stakeholder analysis should identify not only who is affected, but also where conflicts are likely to emerge and how the decision maker should manage those conflicts.

Stakeholders are individuals or groups that can affect or be affected by an organization’s decisions and actions. Stakeholder analysis helps managers identify who is affected, what they care about, how much influence they have, and how the organization should communicate with and respond to them. This is essential for ethical decision-making because a recommendation that ignores affected stakeholders may look efficient in the short run but create resistance, reputational harm, legal risk, or moral failure over time.13Freeman, R. E. (1984). Strategic Management: A Stakeholder Approach. Pitman.

Stakeholder analysis also reflects a shift from a narrow shareholder-only view toward a broader understanding of organizational responsibility. Shareholders remain important, but they are not the only group affected by managerial decisions. Employees, customers, suppliers, communities, regulators, advocacy groups, and the natural environment may also be affected. A strong managerial recommendation does not treat all stakeholder interests as identical, but it does identify them explicitly and explains how the recommended action balances competing obligations.14Shin, S., Lee, J., & Bansal, P. (2022). From a shareholder to stakeholder orientation: Evidence from the analyses of CEO dismissal in large US firms. Strategic Management Journal, 43(7), 1233–1257.

For the consulting project, stakeholder analysis should not be a list of names added after the recommendation has already been chosen. It should shape the analysis itself. If an alternative reduces costs but increases employee harm, customer distrust, or regulatory risk, that tradeoff must be considered before choosing the final recommendation. Similarly, if a stakeholder has low formal power but high moral legitimacy, the manager should still take that stakeholder seriously.

Stakeholder Analysis Frameworks

Stakeholder frameworks help managers organize complex stakeholder relationships. They do not make the decision automatically. Instead, they help decision makers ask better questions about power, interest, legitimacy, urgency, influence, and engagement. Different frameworks are useful at different stages of a decision or project.

Mendelow’s Power-Interest Grid

Mendelow’s grid categorizes stakeholders based on power and interest. Power refers to the stakeholder’s ability to influence the issue or organization. Interest refers to how much the stakeholder is affected by or concerned about the decision. High-power, high-interest stakeholders should be managed closely because they can shape the outcome and care strongly about it. High-power, low-interest stakeholders should be kept satisfied. Low-power, high-interest stakeholders should be kept informed. Low-power, low-interest stakeholders should be monitored with less intensive effort.15Mendelow, A. L. (1981). Environmental Scanning: The Impact of the Stakeholder Concept. Proceedings from the Second International Conference on Information Systems, 407–418.

The Salience Model

The salience model classifies stakeholders based on power, legitimacy, and urgency. Power refers to the ability to influence the organization. Legitimacy refers to whether the stakeholder’s involvement or claim is socially or morally valid. Urgency refers to whether the stakeholder’s claim requires immediate attention. Stakeholders with all three attributes are definitive and should receive high priority. Stakeholder Mapping: Interest-Influence Approach

The interest-influence approach is especially useful for the consulting project because it helps students connect stakeholder impact to managerial action. Interest asks, “How much is this stakeholder affected by the decision?” Influence asks, “How much can this stakeholder affect the decision or its implementation?” Stakeholders who have both high influence and high interest should receive the most careful attention because they are affected by the decision and can affect whether the decision succeeds.17Smith, L. W. (2000). Stakeholder analysis: A pivotal practice of successful projects. Paper presented at Project Management Institute Annual Seminars & Symposium, Houston, TX.

For example, consider a fast-food chain responding to pressure for higher wages. Employees may have high interest because wages directly affect their well-being, and they may have high influence if they can organize, protest, quit, or attract media attention. Executives have high influence because they make strategic and operational decisions. Customers may have medium influence because their purchasing behavior affects revenue. Community groups may have lower formal influence but high interest if wages affect local economic conditions. A good stakeholder analysis explains these distinctions rather than treating every stakeholder as equally affected or equally powerful.

Stakeholder Engagement Assessment Matrix

The Stakeholder Engagement Assessment Matrix is used after stakeholders have been identified and categorized. It assesses whether stakeholders are unaware, resistant, neutral, supportive, or leading. The key is to compare the stakeholder’s current level of engagement with the desired level of engagement. If a stakeholder is currently resistant but needs to become supportive for the decision to succeed, the manager must plan how to bridge that gap through communication, involvement, negotiation, or addressing legitimate concerns.18Riahi, Y. (2017). Project stakeholders: Analysis and management processes. SSRG International Journal of Economics and Management Studies, 4(3), 37–42.

This tool is especially useful during implementation. A recommendation can be analytically strong but still fail if key stakeholders remain resistant or uninformed. For example, if employees are expected to accept new monitoring technology, managers may need to move them from resistant or neutral to at least supportive by explaining the purpose of the system, creating privacy safeguards, and involving employee representatives in implementation.

RACI Matrix

The RACI matrix clarifies roles during implementation. It identifies who is Responsible for doing the work, who is Accountable for final ownership or approval, who should be Consulted before action, and who should be Informed about progress or outcomes. RACI is less useful for analyzing who is affected by a decision, but it is useful once the recommendation becomes an implementation plan.19Good, L. (2024). What is RACI matrix? Project Management.com; Miranda, D., & Watts, R. (2024). What Is A RACI Chart? Forbes Advisor.

For example, if a company adopts a new ethics hotline after employee complaints, HR may be responsible for implementation, the Chief People Officer may be accountable, legal counsel and employee representatives may be consulted, and all employees may be informed. Without this clarity, implementation can fail because everyone assumes someone else is responsible.

Steps for Conducting Stakeholder Analysis

A complete stakeholder analysis should proceed in five steps. First, identify all relevant stakeholders, including internal stakeholders such as employees, managers, executives, shareholders, and board members, and external stakeholders such as customers, suppliers, regulators, communities, advocacy groups, media, competitors, and investors. Second, analyze each stakeholder’s interests and influence. Third, map stakeholders using a framework such as interest-influence mapping. Fourth, develop engagement strategies for each group. Fifth, monitor and review the analysis because stakeholder positions can change as new information emerges or as the issue evolves.17Smith, L. W. (2000). Stakeholder analysis: A pivotal practice of successful projects. Paper presented at Project Management Institute Annual Seminars & Symposium, Houston, TX.

The most common student mistake is to identify only obvious stakeholders. For example, if a company is accused of using biased AI hiring tools, applicants are stakeholders, but so are current employees, HR staff, hiring managers, legal counsel, vendors, regulators, advocacy groups, customers, and shareholders. The analysis should explain how each stakeholder is affected and how they may influence the decision. A stakeholder who lacks formal power may still have moral urgency or reputational influence.

Key Takeaways

Decision-making is a behavioral, ethical, and strategic process. Managers must understand the type of decision they face, choose an appropriate decision model, recognize biases and fallacies, and evaluate how decisions affect stakeholders. Routine decisions may be handled with rules and experience, but novel and ethical decisions require reflective analysis, stakeholder awareness, and careful comparison of alternatives.

Stakeholder analysis improves decision quality because it forces managers to look beyond the preferences of the decision maker and consider who is affected, who has influence, and whose needs require attention. In consulting work, strong recommendations emerge from defining the problem clearly, evaluating alternatives fairly, anticipating risks, and explaining why the selected recommendation is ethically and managerially defensible.

Managerial Insight

Effective managers do not simply make faster decisions. They make better-structured decisions. They know when to rely on routine rules, when to slow down and analyze, when to involve others, when to challenge group consensus, and when to use stakeholder analysis to uncover risks that are not immediately visible.

When making recommendations that address a problem, the most important habit is to work backward from the decision maker’s problem. Identify the decision that must be made, determine the criteria that should guide it, identify affected stakeholders, develop realistic alternatives, evaluate tradeoffs, and recommend one course of action that the decision maker can actually implement.
